## What My Health Says About My Garden

### What I'm Managing

| Condition | How the Garden Helps |
|-----------|---------------------|
| **ADHD (inattentive)** | Gardening is physical, present-tense, sensory-rich — it's one of the few things that quiets the noise. Short tasks (pick herbs, pull weeds, water one bed) are ADHD-friendly dopamine hits. |
| **Anxiety (generalized)** | Grounding — literally. Bare feet in dirt, hands in soil. The garden is a nervous system reset. Growing chamomile, lavender, and lemon balm means my anxiety medicine grows in my yard. |
| **PTSD (chronic)** | The garden is a controlled, safe environment where I have agency. I decide what grows. I decide what stays. That matters when your history includes not having that choice. |
| **GERD / acid reflux** | Growing fennel, chamomile, ginger (in pots), peppermint, and dill — all digestive herbs I can brew fresh instead of reaching for Omeprazole every time. |
| **Hypothyroidism (monitored)** | Ashwagandha (in my SeedTherapy kit) supports thyroid function. Lemon balm supports thyroid regulation. Nettle is nutrient-dense. These are in the apothecary garden plan. |
| **Iron deficiency** | Nettle tea is iron-rich. Dandelion greens (already growing wild). Parsley. Chickweed (already in the yard). The "weeds" in my lawn are literally iron supplements. |
| **PCOS** | Spearmint tea (hormone balance — already growing). Fenugreek (already stocked in bulk for tea). White peony root (already in my tea pantry in pounds). |
| **Hypermobility** | Gentle gardening (raised beds, no deep bending) is joint-friendly movement. The garden keeps me active without high-impact stress. |

### Pharmacogenetics Note

I'm a CYP2C19 intermediate metabolizer — I process certain medications (including my Escitalopram) slower than average. Growing my own medicinal herbs means I can supplement gently and know exactly what's going into my body.
