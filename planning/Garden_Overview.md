## Garden Philosophy

**Sustainable. Self-sufficient. Self-seeding. Natural but helpful. Fun, kitschy, and witchy. Rooted in herbology.**

This yard should get better and wilder every year with less work, not more. Design every bed to eventually take care of itself — and to feed the beds around it.

### What this means in practice

**Let it reseed.** Don't deadhead everything. Poppies, cosmos, zinnias, borage, chamomile, black-eyed susans, coneflowers, bachelor buttons, and dill will all come back on their own if you let the seed heads dry on the plant. By year 3, half the garden plants itself.

**Natives first.** Native perennials (coneflowers, milkweed, bee balm, goldenrod, Joe Pye weed, asters, switchgrass, bluestem) don't need fertilizer, don't need extra water once established, and feed the pollinators that actually live here. The American Meadows order and Prairie Habitat kits are the backbone — annuals are the decoration.

**No synthetic chemicals.** Companion planting for pest control (marigolds near tomatoes, basil near peppers, nasturtiums as trap crops). Neem oil and insecticidal soap only when needed. Let the beneficial insects do their jobs.

**Build soil, don't buy it forever.** Compost, cover crops (buckwheat in the corn circle — already started), mulch leaves in fall instead of bagging them, let spent plants decompose in place over winter.

**Harvest rainwater.** Even one barrel off a downspout cuts watering needs. The frog pond in the herb spiral doubles as irrigation reserve.

**Herbology is the heart of it.** Know what heals. Every plant in this yard has a use beyond looking pretty — learn it, label it, harvest it. This isn't decorative gardening. It's a living apothecary.

**The witchy part is real.** The Alchemist's Pond, the herb spiral, the garden names (Bloomveil, The Gloam, Twilight Hollow, Moonlit Meadow) — this is a garden that has intention built into it. The signs aren't decoration — they're naming the land.

**Fun and kitschy means:** Giant sunflowers. A gourd gate. Glass gem popcorn. A cat garden with bunny tails grass. Pumpkin patches. A tomato obelisk circle. Strawberry popcorn. Don't make it precious — make it alive.

**Welcome the "weeds."** The yard is full of chickweed, speedwell, and other ground-level plants — and that's good. Chickweed is edible (salads, pesto), a living mulch that protects bare soil, and one of the first foods for early pollinators. Speedwell feeds tiny native bees when nothing else is blooming yet. Clover fixes nitrogen. Dandelions are the first bee food of spring. Don't spray them, don't pull them from paths and lawn areas. They're part of the ecosystem. A "life-giving for everyone" yard means the insects, the birds, the toads, the soil microbes, and the chickens-next-door all eat here too.

**Only pull weeds that are actually competing** with something you planted, in the bed where you planted it. Everywhere else, let the ground cover do its thing.

### How the Beds Feed Each Other

The property isn't 15 separate gardens — it's one system. Each bed gives something to the others.

```
WAYWARD MEADOW (back corner)
  │ Pollinators raised here visit every other bed
  │ Native grasses break wind for Wildwood behind fence
  │
WILDWOOD (veggie garden)
  │ Buckwheat cover crop → feeds bees + fixes soil → turned under for corn
  │ Corn circle (Three Sisters) → corn holds beans, beans fix nitrogen, squash shades soil
  │ Compost from spent veggie plants → feeds Bloomveil and Fernwood
  │ Marigolds in veggie rows → repel nematodes + feed soil when turned under
  │
BLOOMVEIL (flower garden)
  │ Cut flowers attract pollinators that cross to Wildwood veggies
  │ Spent annuals → compost pile
  │ Self-seeding annuals spread naturally toward Edgeworth and Wayward
  │
FERNWOOD (shade garden)
  │ Fallen leaves from overhead trees = natural mulch (don't rake)
  │ Hostas and ferns hold moisture in soil → benefits nearby tree roots
  │ Lily-of-the-valley spreads as groundcover → suppresses weeds
  │
HERB SPIRAL / ALCHEMIST'S POND
  │ Frog pond → frogs eat slugs and mosquitoes for the whole yard
  │ Herbs attract beneficial wasps that parasitize tomato hornworms
  │ Overflow water irrigates surrounding beds
  │
CAT GARDEN → Catnip and catmint attract pollinators when blooming
BLUEBERRY & RASPBERRY BED → Shared acidic soil zone, companion plants attract pollinators for fruit set
TOMATO CIRCLE → Tomatoes, peppers, marigolds on obelisks — pollinators cross-visit from nearby beds
```

**The cycle:** Wayward Meadow raises the pollinators. Wildwood grows the food. Bloomveil feeds the soul and the bees. The herb spiral heals you and controls pests. Fernwood recycles what falls. Compost from all beds feeds the next season. Nothing is wasted.
