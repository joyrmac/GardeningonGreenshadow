Your bloodline carries specific plant knowledge from four major traditions. These aren't random herbs—your ancestors grew these because they work, because they're protective, because they heal, because they're sacred.

### CELTIC / BRITISH / IRISH HERITAGE

These are the herbs of Samhain, Beltane, Imbolc, and Lughnasadh. The herbs of the veil-thinning, of ancestral work, of protection.

**Must-Grow (Core Herbs):**
- **Mugwort** — THE ancestral herb. Visions, dreaming, ancestral connection, protection. Your Celtic ancestors used this for ritual and prophecy. Grown everywhere in Celtic lands.
- **St. John's Wort** — Protection, light-bringing, sun magic. Harvested at summer solstice (Midsummer). Burned for protection, dried for winter.
- **Yarrow** — Healing, divination, protection, courage. Used in war, in medicine, in magic. Still considered a "witch herb."
- **Hawthorn** — The fairy tree. Protection, love, boundaries. Plant near property lines. Blooms May 1 (Beltane).
- **Rowan** — Protection, courage, ancestral connection. Berries are powerful. Plant as a tree if possible.
- **Vervain** — Cleansing, protection, sacred herb. Used in Celtic rituals and healing.
- **Sage** — Clearing, protection, wisdom. Your ancestors knew sage.

**Also-Grow (Secondary Herbs):**
- Primrose (spring magic, fairy connection)
- Daisy (healing, innocence, transformation)
- Cowslip (protection, youth)
- Feverfew (protection, healing)
- Thyme (courage, cleansing)
- Rue (protection, cleansing—use cautiously, can cause skin sensitivity)
- Comfrey (healing, protection)
- Plantain (healing, common and powerful)
- Chickweed (healing, gentleness)
- Nettle (protection, strength, grounding)
- Wood betony (protection, grounding, courage)

**Trees to Plant (If Space):**
- Hawthorn
- Rowan
- Oak (if you have room—oak is THE Celtic tree)
- Birch (protection, new beginnings)
- Elder (the fairy tree, protection, healing)

## PART TWO: GARDEN RITUALS & WITCHY PRACTICES

Now that you know what to grow, here's what to **do** with your garden space. These are actual practices your ancestors used, plus modern adaptations.

### ALTAR SPIRALS & SACRED GEOMETRY

**Altar Spiral (What You're Thinking Of):**

This is a spiral pattern made on the ground, often with clay, stones, or plant materials. It's both aesthetic and magical—a walking meditation, an offering space, a focal point for ritual.

**How to Create One:**

1. **Choose Location:** A visible place in your garden where you'll tend it regularly
2. **Size:** 4-8 feet diameter depending on space
3. **Materials:** 
   - Clay (fired or unfired)
   - Stones
   - Shells
   - Crystals
   - Herbs laid fresh
   - Or a combination

4. **Design Options:**
   - **Clay spiral:** Roll clay into long snakes, arrange in spiral pattern
   - **Stone spiral:** Use stones of different sizes/colors arranged spirally
   - **Herb spiral:** Plant herbs spirally (actual growing spiral)
   - **Mixed:** Combine clay center with stone spiral around it

5. **Magical Intention:**
   - Spirals move energy inward (toward center) or outward
   - Inward spiral = calling things in, introspection, drawing down
   - Outward spiral = sending things out, releasing, expansion
   - Which direction calls to you?

**What to Do With It:**
- Leave offerings (flowers, herbs, bread, water)
- Walk it as a meditation
- Sit in center for ritual work
- Place candles at intervals for evening magic
- Change what's in it with seasons

**Seasonal Altar Spiral Evolution:**
- **Spring:** Fresh greens, stones, new growth herbs
- **Summer:** Flowers, bright stones, fire-aligned herbs
- **Fall:** Seeds, harvest materials, dried herbs
- **Winter:** Clay, evergreen branches, white/silver stones

### HERB SPIRALS (Functional + Magical)

An herb spiral is both a growing system AND a ritual object. It's a 3D spiral (usually on a slope or mounded) where you plant herbs that like different moisture/sun levels.

**How to Build One:**

1. **Structure:** 
   - Mound soil in spiral shape, 3-4 feet diameter
   - Or use stones to build spiraling walls (easier, more permanent)
   - Creates height variation: taller at center, slopes down

2. **Planting:**
   - Top (driest): Lavender, rosemary, thyme, sage
   - Middle (moderate): Oregano, marjoram, calendula
   - Bottom (wettest): Mint, lemon balm, comfrey

3. **Magical Integration:**
   - As you plant, speak intentions
   - Each layer represents different energies
   - Walking around it is a moving meditation

### MOON PHASE PLANTING & MAGIC

Your ancestors didn't just plant randomly—they planted by the moon.

**Moon Phase Planting Guide:**

- **New Moon:** Plant seeds of intention, things you want to grow (hidden/internal work)
- **Waxing Moon:** Plant for growth, expansion, bringing things in
- **Full Moon:** Ritual work, charging water/oils, divination, ancestral work
- **Waning Moon:** Plant for release, banishing, clearing; harvest for preservation

**Simple Practice:**
Check the moon phase when you plant or harvest. Just knowing creates intention.

**Ritual Enhancement:**
- Plant mugwort on new moon (ancestral connection)
- Harvest St. John's Wort at full moon (power amplified)
- Clear garden beds on waning moon (release old energy)

### Sacred Trees — Greenshadow Plantings (Decided May 2026)

#### 🌲 Eastern Red Cedar 'Brodie' (*Juniperus virginiana* 'Brodie')

- **Tradition:** Germanic / Northern — protection, purification, winter survival, ancestral burning
- **Cultivar reasoning:** Female (= blue berries for birds and ancestral berry use), narrow upright form (15–20 ft tall × 6–8 ft wide), most balanced size for harvest without overwhelming the bed
- **Location:** Wayward Meadow, north edge — between the Cat Garden's tulip magnolia and the Gloam's southern magnolia. Walking distance to the Fire Pit on the south fence
- **Why this spot:**
  - Full sun (south of the trees, sunlight unblocked)
  - Drought-tolerant + vole-resistant — ideal for the meadow border
  - **Short walk to Fire Pit** for ritual burning of harvested branches
  - Far from front-yard hawthorn (cedar-apple rust mitigated by ~250 ft + house in middle)
- **Planting requirements:**
  - 8–10 ft trunk-to-trunk from each existing magnolia (NOT in the literal 10 ft gap if they're really that close — verify field distance first)
  - Sharp grit ring at base (vole insurance)
  - No hardware cloth basket needed (juniper is vole-resistant)
  - Container-grown 5-gallon stock from local NC nursery
- **Companion plantings at base:**
  - Wormwood (Germanic/EE ancestral pairing — silver foliage against juniper green)
  - Yarrow (drought-tough, native, ancestral all four traditions)
  - Aromatic Aster (#210 from American Meadows order — fall purple-blue blooms)
  - Pale Purple Coneflower (#218 — drought-tough native)
  - Small Calico Aster cluster (#191)
  - Little Bluestem (#211 — native bunchgrass with bronze fall color)
- **Timeline:** Buy + plant fall 2026 (Oct–Nov ideal). First small harvest year 2; meaningful harvest year 3+. Cut branches dry 6–12 months before burning.

#### 🌳 'Winter King' Green Hawthorn (*Crataegus viridis* 'Winter King')

- **Tradition:** Celtic / British / Irish — fairy tree, threshold/boundary protection
- **Cultivar reasoning:** **Rust-resistant** (chosen specifically because of the cedar on-property), Celtic ancestral resonance intact, white Beltane-time blooms, brilliant **persistent red berries** through winter (food for birds + traditional jelly + Samhain harvest)
- **Location:** Front yard, west side of the house — threshold/entry position where guests arrive
- **Why this spot:**
  - Maximum diagonal separation from cedar (~250+ ft, with house as wind/spore barrier)

- **Planting requirements:**
  - 20–25 ft mature size — give it room from house, walkway, and any power lines
  - Single-trunk specimen (better for front-yard formal placement)
  - Container-grown stock from local NC nursery (more common than this cultivar may sound)
- **Timeline:** Buy + plant fall 2026 (Oct–Nov ideal). Beltane blooms by year 2–3; full structure by year 5.
