# Honeyglow Patch (Pollinator Garden)

> The pollinator haven inside Wildwood. A self-seeding, low-maintenance bed that feeds bees, butterflies, and hummingbirds all season long.

**Sign:** "HONEYGLOW PATCH -- Buzzing with beauty"

---

## Location

Inside Wildwood, near the Gourd Gate arbor. Bordered by lawn on one side and adjacent Wildwood growing beds on the other. Wire support grids over portions of the bed. Landscape edging separates it from the lawn.

## Summer Character (from 2025 photos)

At peak summer the bed is a wall of color:
- **Zinnias** blanket the foreground -- pink, red, orange, salmon, hot pink
- **Purple larkspur** spikes rise above the zinnias throughout
- **Coneflowers** (Echinacea purpurea, pink) visible in the back/edges
- **Feathery larkspur/cosmos foliage** fills gaps
- Wire support grid helps keep tall plants upright
- Crepe myrtle blooming white overhead

The bed is lush but chaotic -- plants crowd each other and spill beyond the edging.

## Bee Balm Relocation Plan

Divisions from Honeyglow thinning. Buried nursery pots (cut bottom out, rim 1" above soil) to contain runners. Divide annually each spring.

| Location | Pot Size | Why |
|----------|----------|-----|
| Wayward Meadow | None (direct plant) | Let it naturalize freely |
| Between Honeyglow and veggie beds | 5-gallon (12") | Main pollinator stepping stone to food crops |
| Gourd Gate entrance | 5-gallon (12") | Beacon for squash/pumpkin pollination |
| Near melon/watermelon patch | 3-gallon (10") | Tucked at edge, draws bees for fruit set |
| Near blueberry barrels | 3-gallon (10") | Attracts native bees for buzz pollination |
| Edgeworth Corner | 10" pot | One contained clump, fits the dense grid |
| Existing pots (from last year) | Keep as-is | Set on pavers, not bare soil -- roots escape drainage holes |

## Makeover Notes

**Problems to address:**
- Zinnias outcompete everything else by midsummer
- Rocket larkspur self-seeds beyond bed boundaries
- Bed is overstuffed -- plants can't breathe
- No clear structure or height layering

**Possible strategies:**
- Thin zinnias aggressively in spring or confine to one section
- Pull larkspur seedlings early where they're not wanted
- Create zones: tall perennials in back, mid-height in middle, low-growers at edges
- Add more native perennials (from recommendations above) to build a backbone that doesn't need replanting each year

## Connection to Wildwood Ecosystem

The Honeyglow Patch is Wildwood's pollinator engine. Bees and butterflies feeding here cross-visit vegetable beds, the Potion Patch, and the cut flower garden. From `planning/Garden_Overview.md`:

> "Wayward Meadow raises the pollinators. Wildwood grows the food. Bloomveil feeds the soul and the bees."

Honeyglow is where Wildwood does its part -- keeping pollinators fed and close to the food crops.
