# Alchemist Pond — Plant Guide

> What to plant in and around the pond, where to put it, and how to keep it alive.

---

## The Rules

- **Native plants first.** They support local frog species and wildlife better than exotics.
- **No fertilizer in or near the pond.** Fertilizer = algae bloom = dead frogs.
- **Contain aggressive spreaders.** Plant in pots sunk into the shelves, not directly in the gravel.
- **Some algae is good.** It's food for tadpoles. Don't panic.

---

## Water Plants — By Location

### SHELF 1 (6" Shallow Edge) — Marginal Plants

These sit with their roots in water and their foliage above. They live on the shallowest shelf.

| Plant | Height | Why | Planting Notes |
|-------|--------|-----|----------------|
| **Blue Flag Iris** (*Iris versicolor*) | 24-36" | Native, gorgeous blue-purple flowers (May-June), frog eggs attach to underwater stems | Plant in a mesh pot sunk into the shelf. 6" of water over the roots. Full sun to part shade. |
| **Water Mint** (*Mentha aquatica*) | 6-12" | Fragrant, ties the pond to the herb spiral, frog-safe, pollinator-friendly | **Plant in a pot — it spreads aggressively.** Sink pot into Shelf 1 on the herb spiral side. |
| **Native Sedges/Rushes** | 12-24" | Natural edge look, tadpole cover, erosion control | Plant in clusters of 3-5 along the edge. Soft rush (*Juncus effusus*) is a great native choice. |

### SHELF 2 (12" Middle) — Taller Water Plants

These need more water depth and provide mid-level structure.

| Plant | Height | Why | Planting Notes |
|-------|--------|-----|----------------|
| **Dwarf Cattail** (*Typha minima*) | 10-18" | Miniature version won't take over, tadpole shelter, classic pond look | Plant in a mesh pot on Shelf 2. Regular cattails get 6+ ft and spread — ONLY use the dwarf variety. |
| **Pickerelweed** (*Pontederia cordata*) | 24-36" | Native, blue flower spikes, loved by dragonflies and frogs | Optional addition — plant in pot on Shelf 2 if you want more blue flowers. |

### FLOATING (Deep Zone Surface)

These float freely on the surface. They shade the water, reduce algae, and give tadpoles cover.

| Plant | Size | Why | Notes |
|-------|------|-----|-------|
| **Water Lettuce** (*Pistia stratiotes*) | 4-6" rosettes | Floating shade, tadpole hiding spots, tropical look | Just drop them on the surface. They multiply. Remove excess if they cover more than 60% of the surface. |
| **Duckweed** (*Lemna minor*) | Tiny (1/4") | Tadpole food, nutrient absorption, shade | Multiplies fast. Scoop out excess with a net if it takes over. A little goes a long way. |

### POND EDGE (On the Rim, Not In the Water)

These grow on land but trail or creep toward the water, softening the transition from garden to pond.

| Plant | Type | Why | Notes |
|-------|------|-----|-------|
| **Creeping Jenny** (*Lysimachia nummularia*) | Trailing groundcover | You already grow this — trail cuttings over the rim rocks into the water. Golden-green, beautiful. | Take cuttings from your porch planter. Free. |
| **Moss** | Groundcover | Colonizes damp rocks naturally. Makes the pond look ancient. | Mist the rim rocks regularly to encourage growth. Or transplant moss from elsewhere in the yard. |
| **Ferns** (any shade-tolerant variety) | Backdrop | If the herb spiral side is shady, a small fern behind the pond adds forest floor texture. | Optional — you have plenty of fern varieties from the Fernwood haul. |

## Planting Method (For Potted Water Plants)

Most water plants should be planted in **mesh aquatic plant pots** (also called pond baskets), NOT directly in the gravel. This contains their roots and prevents aggressive spreading.

### How to Plant in Mesh Pots

1. **Get mesh aquatic pots** (various sizes, $2-5 each from pond suppliers)
2. **Fill pot with aquatic planting media** (heavy clay soil or specialized aquatic soil — NOT regular potting mix, which floats)
3. **Plant the plant** at the same depth it was growing in its nursery pot
4. **Top with a 1-inch layer of pea gravel** — this holds the soil down so it doesn't cloud the water
5. **Lower the pot onto the shelf** at the correct depth
6. Done. The roots grow through the mesh into the gravel but the main root mass stays contained.

### Depth Guide

| Plant | Water Depth Over Soil Surface |
|-------|------|
| Blue Flag Iris | 2-6 inches |
| Water Mint | 1-4 inches |
| Sedges/Rushes | 1-4 inches |
| Dwarf Cattail | 4-8 inches |
| Pickerelweed | 4-8 inches |
| Water Lettuce | Floating (no pot needed) |
| Duckweed | Floating (no pot needed) |

## When to Plant

| Plant | Best Time | Notes |
|-------|-----------|-------|
| Blue Flag Iris | Spring (April-May) | Plant when water temps are warming |
| Dwarf Cattail | Spring (May) | After last frost |
| Water Mint | Spring (April-May) | Hardy, can go in early |
| Sedges/Rushes | Spring (April-May) | Native, tough |
| Water Lettuce | After last frost (late April+) | Tropical — will die in frost |
| Duckweed | Anytime water is warm | It doesn't care |
| Creeping Jenny | Spring-Summer | Take cuttings, root in damp soil at edge |

## What NOT to Plant

| Plant | Why Not |
|-------|---------|
| **Regular Cattails** (*Typha latifolia*) | 6-8 ft tall, spreads aggressively, will take over the entire pond in one season |
| **Water Hyacinth** | Invasive in NC — illegal to plant in many areas |
| **Parrot Feather** | Invasive, illegal in many states |
| **Yellow Flag Iris** (*Iris pseudacorus*) | Invasive in NC — use Blue Flag instead (native) |
| **Lotus** | Too big for this pond, needs 18"+ of water and full sun |

## Year 1 vs. Year 2+ Expectations

### Year 1
- Plants are small and establishing
- Water may be murky or algae-green at first (normal)
- Duckweed and water lettuce will fill in fastest
- Blue flag iris may not bloom until year 2
- Frogs will find the pond quickly — eggs and tadpoles likely within weeks

### Year 2+
- Iris blooms (May-June), cattails fill out
- Creeping jenny trails beautifully over the rim
- Moss covers the rocks
- Self-sustaining ecosystem with minimal input
- Frogs return every spring to breed

## Maintenance Summary

- **Spring:** Divide plants that have outgrown their pots. Add new floating plants if winter killed them.
- **Summer:** Remove excess duckweed/water lettuce if it covers more than 60% of the surface. Top off water during hot spells.
- **Fall:** Let some leaves fall in (tadpole food/frog habitat). Remove excess if they're smothering the water.
- **Winter:** Leave the pond alone. Frogs are hibernating in the deep zone mud. Don't break ice — the vibrations can kill hibernating frogs. If you need a hole in the ice for gas exchange, pour hot water to melt a spot.
