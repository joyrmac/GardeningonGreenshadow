# Hellebore Care Guide — Fernwood Ginkgo Grove

> **Location:** Under the ginkgo tree at the entrance to Fernwood
> **Count:** 6 hellebores (varieties unknown — possibly includes 'Dark and Handsome' and 'Amber Gem' from Plant Delights order)
> **Planted:** April 2026

---

## What Hellebores Are

Hellebores (*Helleborus* spp.) are one of the first perennials to bloom — often January through March, sometimes even in snow. They're sometimes called Lenten roses or Christmas roses. They're shade-loving, deer-resistant, drought-tolerant once established, and essentially indestructible.

Under a ginkgo tree at the entrance to Fernwood is a perfect spot. Deciduous trees like ginkgo give hellebores exactly what they want: winter/early spring sun (when the tree is bare and the hellebores are blooming) and summer shade (when the tree leafs out and the hellebores are resting).

---

## Year 1: Establishment (This Year)

The most important thing this year is getting the roots established. Don't expect a big show — they're settling in.

### Watering
- **First 2-3 weeks:** Water every 2-3 days (they're transplants, roots need to establish)
- **After that:** Once a week through summer, especially during hot/dry spells
- **Fall/winter:** Back off to natural rainfall unless it's very dry
- **Year 2+:** Hellebores are drought-tolerant once established. You can mostly forget about them.
- **Water at the base, not overhead.** Wet foliage in shade invites fungal problems.

### Mulch
- 2-3 inches of leaf mold, compost, or shredded leaves around each plant
- **Keep mulch 3-4 inches away from the crown** (the center where the stems emerge from the ground). Mulch piled on the crown causes rot.
- Ginkgo leaves make excellent mulch — in fall when they drop, just leave them around the hellebores. They break down fast and feed the soil.

### Feeding
- This year: the worm castings you already applied to Fernwood are perfect. Nothing else needed.
- Going forward: one application of compost or balanced slow-release fertilizer in late winter (February) each year is plenty.

### What to Expect
- Flowers this year: maybe a few, maybe none. That's normal for transplants.
- Leaves should stay green and healthy through summer.
- In late fall, foliage may look tired or tattered. Leave it — it protects the crown over winter.

## Year 2+: The Routine

### Late Winter (January-February)

**This is when hellebores shine.**

- **Cut off all the old foliage** in late January or early February, before the new flower stems emerge
- Cut each old leaf stem at the base, right above the crown
- This sounds scary but it's the most important maintenance task — the old leaves hide the flowers and harbor disease
- Within days to weeks, you'll see new flower buds pushing up from the crown
- Blooms last 2-3 months. They fade slowly, turning green — still beautiful

### Spring (March-May)
- New foliage grows as flowers fade
- Top-dress with compost if you want (not required)
- Leave faded flower stems if you want seeds (hellebores self-sow). Cut them off if you don't want seedlings popping up.

### Summer (June-August)
- Basically ignore them. They sit quietly in the shade.
- Water during extended drought (2+ weeks no rain)
- Leaves should stay green and leathery
- No deadheading needed, no fussing needed

### Fall (September-November)
- Leaves may look worn by late fall. Leave them — they protect the crown over winter.
- Let ginkgo leaves fall around them as natural mulch

## Spacing & Arrangement

With 6 hellebores under one ginkgo tree:

- **Space 18-24 inches apart** — they get about 18 inches wide at maturity
- **Plant in a loose arc or cluster** under the ginkgo canopy, not in a straight line
- **Face the flowers toward the path** — hellebore flowers nod downward, so plant them where you'll be looking up into them from below, or along the entrance path where you walk past at eye level
- **Stagger depths** — don't plant them all at the same distance from the trunk. Some closer (12-18" from trunk), some farther out (3-4 ft). This looks natural and gives them different light conditions as the ginkgo leafs out.

## Troubleshooting

| Problem | Cause | Fix |
|---------|-------|-----|
| **Drooping/wilting** | Overwatering or waterlogged soil | Check drainage. Hellebores hate soggy roots. Add grit/compost to improve drainage if needed. |
| **Black spots on leaves** | Hellebore leaf spot (fungal) | Cut off affected leaves. Improve air circulation. Remove old foliage in late winter (prevents this). |
| **No flowers** | Too young, or old foliage is smothering the buds | Give transplants a full year. Cut old foliage in late January to expose emerging buds. |
| **Flowers face down (can't see them)** | That's just what hellebores do | Plant on a slope or raised area so you look up into them. Or cut a few stems and float the flowers face-up in a bowl of water indoors. |
| **Yellow leaves in summer** | Too much sun or too dry | Should be fine under the ginkgo. If yellowing, water more deeply. |
| **Tiny seedlings popping up nearby** | Self-sowing (hellebores cross-pollinate freely) | Keep them! Free plants. Or transplant them elsewhere. The seedlings won't be identical to the parents — they'll be unique hybrids. |
| **Crown rot** | Mulch piled on the crown or waterlogged soil | Pull mulch away from the crown. Improve drainage. |
| **Slugs eating leaves** | Slugs love shade gardens | Beer traps. Check under leaves after rain. |

## The Ginkgo + Hellebore Partnership

This is actually one of the best tree/perennial combos in shade gardening:

- **Winter-early spring:** Ginkgo is bare, sun reaches the ground, hellebores bloom in full light. This is their season.
- **Late spring-summer:** Ginkgo leafs out, creates shade, hellebores rest in cool green quiet. They don't need sun now.
- **Fall:** Ginkgo drops ALL its leaves at once (ginkgos do this — the entire canopy drops in about 3 days). The fan-shaped leaves make perfect mulch for the hellebores. Leave them.
- **Repeat.**

The ginkgo gives the hellebores exactly the right light at exactly the right time. You picked the perfect spot.

| **Water** | Moderate year 1, drought-tolerant year 2+ |
