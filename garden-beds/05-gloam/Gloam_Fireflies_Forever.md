## Why a Southern Magnolia Is Tough for Most Plants

A full-grown southern magnolia (*Magnolia grandiflora*):

- **Shallow, dense, aggressive surface roots** — terrible for digging or root competition
- **Sheds leathery leaves year-round** (evergreen) that smother smaller plants
- **Casts deep year-round shade** (unlike deciduous trees, no winter sun gets through)
- **Intercepts rain** — soil under canopy stays drier than expected
- **Leaf litter is acidic and slow to decompose** — forms a thick mat
- **Allelopathic tendencies** — magnolia leaf compounds suppress some understory plants

**Plants that DO thrive here are dry-shade specialists.** That's the corrected plant list below.

### Companion Notes

- **Pine straw / pine needle mulch** — perfect for the Gloam. Acidic, mimics forest floor, breaks down slowly, won't smother. Better than wood chips here.
- **Plant on the south/southeast side** of the magnolia where filtered afternoon light penetrates. The deep north-side shade is harshest.
- **Stay 3+ ft from the magnolia trunk** — root competition is too intense in the inner zone.
- **Water the first 1–2 years** while plants establish. After that, tough as nails.

## What's Actually for Fireflies?

The collection name "Fireflies Forever" is suggestive but the original plant list wasn't actually firefly-targeted. **What fireflies actually want:**

- **Leaf litter on the ground** (don't rake the magnolia leaves — they're firefly nursery!)
- **Dappled darkness** at twilight (no bright outdoor lights nearby)
- **Some moisture** — small water dish, mossy spot, or proximity to the frog pond
- **Native plants** providing leaf-mat habitat for larvae (which feed on slugs/snails)
- **Slow-decay surface debris** (logs, sticks)

**Practical firefly habitat additions to the Gloam:**

- [ ] Leave magnolia leaves where they fall (they're already doing the work)
- [ ] Tuck a small flat dish of water in the dappled shade (refresh weekly)
- [ ] Add a small log or two as woody debris (slug habitat = firefly food)
- [ ] No outdoor lighting in the Gloam zone — preserves twilight darkness

## Planting Notes

- **Best planting time:** Fall (Oct–Nov in NC) — cool soil, plants establish over winter
- **Spring planting works** but requires diligent watering through the first summer
- **Mulch with pine needles** 2–3" deep, kept 3" back from trunks
- **Don't amend with compost heavily** under the magnolia — magnolia roots will outcompete and the new plants will cycle nutrients faster than they can use them
- **Skip drip irrigation directly under the magnolia** — surface roots get too aggressive when watered
