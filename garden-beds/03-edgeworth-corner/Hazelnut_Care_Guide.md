# Contorted Hazelnut (Harry Lauder's Walking Stick) — Rehabilitation Guide

> **Location:** Near Edgeworth Corner, between the patio and the garden
> **Variety:** *Corylus avellana* 'Contorta' — ornamental contorted/corkscrew hazelnut
> **Current State:** Neglected, ~30-40% dead wood, only leafing out on west/south side, severely light-limited (2-3 hours direct sun)
> **Goal:** Rehabilitate what's alive, remove what's dead, maximize health in a less-than-ideal spot

---

## The Situation

This tree has never been pruned, rarely watered, and gets only about 2-3 hours of direct sun (roughly noon to 2-3pm). The east side is mostly dead branches. The green growth is concentrated on the west/south side where it catches what sun it gets. The previous owners didn't maintain it. It can't be moved.

**The plan is not to make it a perfect tree. The plan is to make it the best version of itself in the spot it's in.**

---

## What This Tree Is

Harry Lauder's Walking Stick is a **grafted ornamental hazelnut** grown for its dramatically twisted, corkscrew branches. It's not really a nut-producing tree — it's a sculpture that happens to be alive.

Key facts:
- **Grafted:** The twisted top is grafted onto a regular (straight-growing) hazelnut rootstock. The rootstock WILL send up straight suckers that steal energy. This is the #1 maintenance task.
- **Deciduous:** Drops leaves in fall. The twisted bare branches in winter are actually the main show — they look incredible against snow or a winter sky.
- **Size:** Typically 8-10 ft tall and wide at maturity.
- **Sun:** Prefers full sun (6+ hours). Tolerates part shade but thins out and dies back in heavy shade.
- **Water:** Moderate — needs consistent moisture, especially in summer heat.
- **Zone:** Hardy to Zone 4. NC Zone 7b is fine temperature-wise.

## Phase 1: Prune (Do This First — April is Fine)

### Step 1: Remove All Dead Wood

Go through the entire tree and cut out every branch that is:
- Completely bare with no buds or leaves
- Brittle and snaps when bent (live branches are flexible)
- Gray/dried out bark that peels off easily

**How to check if a branch is dead:** Scratch the bark with your thumbnail. If it's green underneath = alive. If it's brown/dry underneath = dead. Cut it.

**Where to cut:** Follow the dead branch back to where it meets a living branch or the trunk. Cut just outside the branch collar (the slightly swollen ring where the branch meets the trunk). Don't leave long stubs. Don't cut flush with the trunk either — the collar is the tree's healing zone.

**Expect to remove a lot.** That east side is probably 80-90% dead. That's okay — removing dead wood doesn't hurt the tree, it helps it stop wasting energy trying to push sap into branches that will never leaf out.

### Step 2: Remove Rootstock Suckers

Look at the base of the tree for any branches that grow **STRAIGHT** instead of twisted. These are suckers from the rootstock — they're a different plant than the contorted top.

- **Straight growth = rootstock = cut it off at the base**
- **Twisted/curled growth = the good stuff = keep it**

Suckers are more vigorous than the contorted variety. If you don't remove them, they'll eventually dominate and you'll lose the contorted character entirely.

Check for suckers **every month** during growing season. They come back.

### Step 3: Thin Crossing Branches

After removing dead wood and suckers, look at what's left:
- Where two living branches cross or rub against each other, remove the weaker one
- Where branches grow inward toward the center, remove them to open up airflow
- The goal: light and air should be able to reach the interior

### Step 4: Shape What's Left

After cleaning up, the tree will probably look lopsided — more green on the west/south, bare framework on the east. That's reality with the light situation.

Options:
- **Embrace the asymmetry.** A one-sided tree with dramatic twisted bare branches on one side and lush foliage on the other is actually striking. Lean into it.
- **Reduce the bare side.** If the dead framework on the east side bothers you, cut it back further to balance the silhouette. Less tree but more intentional looking.

### Tools Needed
- Bypass hand pruners (for branches under 1/2 inch)
- Bypass loppers (for branches 1/2 to 1.5 inches)
- Pruning saw (for anything larger)
- Rubbing alcohol or bleach wipes to clean blades between cuts (prevents spreading disease)

## Phase 2: Feed (After Pruning)

### Clear the Base
- Pull back ground cover and weeds from within 18 inches of the trunk
- The trunk base needs to breathe — moisture and vegetation piled against it invites rot and pests

### Amend the Soil
1. **Spread 2-3 inches of compost** in a ring from 6 inches away from the trunk out to the drip line (where the branches end overhead)
2. **Add worm castings** on top of the compost — same as what you did in Fernwood
3. **Optionally:** Sprinkle a balanced slow-release fertilizer (10-10-10) according to package directions. One application in spring is enough.

### Mulch
- Add 2-3 inches of wood chip or leaf mold mulch over the compost
- Keep mulch 4-6 inches away from the trunk (no mulch volcanos)
- Extend mulch out to the drip line
- This retains moisture, feeds the soil, and suppresses the ground cover from creeping back

## Phase 3: Water (Start Now and Keep Going)

**This tree has never been deliberately watered. That changes today.**

### Watering Schedule
| Season | Frequency | Method | Duration |
|--------|-----------|--------|----------|
| **Spring (Mar-May)** | Every 2 weeks if no rain | Hose at base, slow trickle | 30-45 min |
| **Summer (Jun-Aug)** | Every week | Hose at base, slow trickle | 45-60 min |
| **Fall (Sep-Nov)** | Every 2-3 weeks if dry | Hose at base | 30 min |
| **Winter (Dec-Feb)** | Only during dry spells | Hose at base | 20 min |

### How to Water
- Lay the hose at the base of the tree with a slow trickle (not a spray)
- Let it run for 30-60 minutes so water soaks deep into the root zone
- Or use a soaker hose in a ring around the drip line
- Water at the base, not on the leaves
- Early morning is best; evening is fine

### The Point
Deep, infrequent watering is better than frequent shallow sprinkles. You want the water to soak 12-18 inches into the soil where the roots are. The "sometimes from the sprinklers" was only wetting the top inch — the roots never got it.

---

## Phase 4: Maximize Light (Since You Can't Move It)

The tree gets about 2-3 hours of direct sun (noon to 2-3pm). That's not ideal. Here's what you can do without moving the tree:

### Option A: Open the Canopy Above
- Look at what's shading the hazelnut — tall trees to the east and west blocking morning and afternoon sun
- If any of those trees have low branches that could be removed (limbed up), it would let more light through to the hazelnut
- Even gaining 1-2 more hours of sun would make a noticeable difference
- This is the single most impactful thing you can do

### Option B: Reflective Surfaces
- If there's a light-colored wall, fence, or structure nearby, it bounces light back toward the tree
- The patio stones also reflect some light — that may already be helping the south-facing side

### Option C: Accept the Limitation
- 2-3 hours of sun means the tree will never be a full, dense canopy all the way around
- But it CAN be a healthy, dramatic, partially-leafed tree with incredible winter branch structure
- The twisted bare branches on the shaded side are actually beautiful in their own right — especially in winter when everything else is bare too

## Phase 5: Ongoing Maintenance

### Monthly (Growing Season)
- [ ] Check for and remove rootstock suckers (straight growth from the base)
- [ ] Water deeply once a week in summer, every 2 weeks in spring/fall
- [ ] Monitor for new leaf growth on previously bare branches (signs of recovery)

### Annually (Late Winter / Early Spring)
- [ ] Prune: remove any new dead wood, crossing branches, and suckers
- [ ] Feed: fresh compost + worm castings around the drip line
- [ ] Refresh mulch to 2-3 inches
- [ ] Check the trunk base is clear of ground cover buildup

### Watch For
| Issue | What It Looks Like | What to Do |
|-------|-------------------|------------|
| **Rootstock suckers** | Straight branches growing from the base | Cut off immediately at the base. Check monthly. |
| **Eastern filbert blight** | Black, raised bumps (cankers) on branches, branch dieback | Cut affected branches 2-3 ft below the canker. Sterilize tools between cuts. Serious but manageable. |
| **Japanese beetles** | Skeletonized leaves (eaten between the veins) | Hand-pick into soapy water. Neem oil spray if heavy. Usually cosmetic, not fatal. |
| **Powdery mildew** | White powder on leaves, especially in humid weather | Improve air circulation (pruning helps). Usually cosmetic in hazelnuts. |
| **Leaf scorch** | Brown crispy leaf edges in summer | Drought stress — water more deeply. |

## What Recovery Looks Like

### Month 1-2 (After Pruning + Feeding + Watering)
- The tree will look more open and possibly more bare (you removed a lot of dead wood)
- This is normal and good — you revealed the truth of what's alive
- New growth may push out on branches that previously couldn't get light

### By End of Summer
- The living side should look fuller and healthier
- Some previously dormant buds on interior branches may wake up with better light and water
- You may see new twiggy growth on branches you thought were marginal

### Year 2
- With consistent care (water, feeding, sucker removal), the living portion should be noticeably more vigorous
- The twisted branch structure on the bare side becomes a feature, not a flaw — especially in winter

### Year 3+
- The tree is in maintenance mode
- It may never be a full 360-degree canopy in this light, but it will be healthy, dramatic, and thriving on the side that gets sun

## The Story of This Tree

> *Contorted hazelnuts are one of the weirdest, most beautiful trees you can grow. Every branch twists and curls like it's reaching for something it can't quite find. In winter, when the leaves drop, the bare branches against a gray sky look like a drawing someone made in a fever dream. This tree has been ignored for years and it's still alive — still pushing green on the side that gets light, still twisting, still trying. Give it water, food, and a good pruning, and watch what it does when someone finally pays attention to it.*
