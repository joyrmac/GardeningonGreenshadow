### Basic Seed Starting Setup

You don't need a greenhouse. You need:

1. **Containers:** Seed trays, egg cartons, or small pots with drainage holes
2. **Soil:** Seed-starting mix (NOT garden soil — it's too heavy and has pathogens)
3. **Light:** A sunny south-facing window or a basic grow light (6500K, 14-16 hours/day)
4. **Water:** Bottom-water (set trays in a dish of water and let soil wick it up) — prevents damping off
5. **Warmth:** Most seeds germinate between 65-75°F. A heating mat helps but isn't required.

### Hardening Off (The Step Everyone Skips)

Before transplanting seedlings outside, they need to adjust to outdoor conditions over 7-10 days:

| Day | Exposure |
|-----|---------|
| Days 1-3 | 2-3 hours in shade, sheltered from wind |
| Days 4-5 | 4-5 hours with some direct sun |
| Days 6-7 | 6+ hours of direct sun |
| Days 8-10 | Leave outside overnight (if no frost) |

Skip this step and your seedlings will wilt, sunburn, or die. It's annoying but non-negotiable.

## Planting Day Basics

### Bloomveil Zone-by-Zone Planting Order

Plant in this order for the smoothest workflow:

1. **Twilight Hollow** (cool-season plants first: Asters, Lavender, Salvia)
2. **Moonlit Meadow** (Poppies can go early; Cosmos and Chamomile after frost)
3. **Spice Drift** (Coneflowers and Rudbeckia are hardy — early spring)
4. **Berry Patch** (Dahlias are frost-sensitive — wait until late April)
5. **Sunrise Cloud** (Sunflowers and warm-season Zinnias — last, after soil warms)

### Spacing Matters

Your plant list specifies spacing (6"-24") for each plant. Common mistakes:
- **Too close:** Plants compete for light and water, get leggy, develop mold
- **Too far:** Bare soil invites weeds and dries out faster
- **Follow your CSV data** — those spacings are specific to each cultivar

### Mulch Everything

After planting, add 2-3 inches of mulch (shredded bark, straw, or leaves) around plants:
- Suppresses weeds
- Retains moisture
- Regulates soil temperature
- Looks intentional

Leave a 1" gap around stems to prevent rot.

## Ongoing Maintenance (The Weekly Walk-Through)

Once a week during growing season (April–October), walk through both gardens with this checklist:

### The 10-Minute Garden Check

- [ ] **Water check:** Stick your finger 1" into soil. Dry? Water deeply. Moist? Skip it.
- [ ] **Deadhead:** Snip spent flowers at the base of the stem. This redirects energy into new blooms.
- [ ] **Pest scan:** Look under leaves for aphids, on stems for powdery mildew, around base for slugs.
- [ ] **Weed pull:** Get them small. A 2-minute pull now saves a 30-minute battle later.
- [ ] **Support check:** Are tall plants (Sunflowers, Dahlias, Joe Pye Weed) leaning? Stake them.
- [ ] **Joy check:** What's blooming? What's beautiful? Take a picture. This is the payoff.

## Watering Guide

| Rule | Why |
|------|-----|
| Water deeply, less often | Encourages deep root growth. Shallow frequent watering = shallow roots. |
| Water in the morning | Leaves dry by afternoon, preventing fungal disease. |
| Water the soil, not the leaves | Wet leaves = mildew. Soaker hoses and drip irrigation are ideal. |
| 1 inch per week | The general rule for most garden plants in your zone. |
| Container plants need more | Pots dry out faster than ground beds. Check daily in summer. |

## Dealing With Common Problems

| Problem | What's Happening | Fix |
|---------|-----------------|-----|
| Yellow leaves (bottom up) | Nitrogen deficiency or overwatering | Ease up on water; add compost or balanced fertilizer |
| White powder on leaves | Powdery mildew | Improve air circulation; spray with diluted neem oil |
| Holes in leaves | Caterpillars or beetles | Hand-pick if few; BT spray for caterpillars; neem oil for beetles |
| Aphids (tiny green/black bugs) | Sap-sucking pests | Blast with hose water; ladybugs eat them; neem oil spray |
| Plants leggy and stretched | Not enough light | Can't fix in current spot; note for next year's layout |
| Nothing is blooming | Too much nitrogen OR not enough sun | Stop fertilizing; reassess sun exposure in that zone |

## Seed Saving (August – October)

Free seeds for next year from your own garden:

1. **Let some flowers go to seed** — don't deadhead everything
2. **Wait until seed heads are dry and brown** on the plant
3. **Cut the seed head and shake into a paper bag**
4. **Label and store** in a cool, dry place (envelope in a mason jar works)

**Easiest to save:** Zinnias, Cosmos, Marigolds, Sunflowers, Coneflowers
**Harder (but doable):** Chamomile, Poppies (tiny seeds)
**Don't bother saving:** Dahlias (propagate by dividing tubers in fall)
