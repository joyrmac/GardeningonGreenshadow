# How to Plant Everything

> Practical planting instructions -- from seed starting indoors to direct sowing to transplanting. No fluff.

---

## Starting Seeds Indoors (6-8 Weeks Before Last Frost)

### You Need

1. **Containers:** Seed trays, egg cartons, or small pots WITH drainage holes
2. **Soil:** Seed-starting mix (NOT garden soil -- it's too heavy and carries disease)
3. **Light:** South-facing window OR a grow light (6500K, 14-16 hours/day). Without enough light, seedlings get leggy and weak
4. **Water:** Bottom-water ONLY (set trays in a dish of water, let soil wick it up). Top watering causes damping off (seedling death)
5. **Warmth:** 65-75F. A heating mat helps germination speed but isn't required

### What to Start Indoors vs. Direct Sow

**Start indoors (they need a head start):**
- Tomatoes, Peppers (SLOW -- start 8-10 weeks before last frost)
- Dahlias (tubers in pots, 4-6 weeks before last frost)
- Snapdragons, Foxglove, Strawflower, Celosia (slow germinators)
- Lavender (notoriously slow -- start 10-12 weeks early)
- Lemongrass, Citronella (tropical -- need long warm season)

**Direct sow outdoors (they hate transplanting):**
- Carrots, Radishes, Beets (root crops don't transplant well)
- Corn, Beans, Peas (fast-growing, resent root disturbance)
- Poppies (need cold soil, scatter on snow or cold ground)
- Cosmos, Zinnias, Sunflowers (fast germinators, do fine direct-sown)
- Nasturtiums (soak seeds overnight, direct sow)
- All wildflower mixes (broadcast directly)

**Either way works:**
- Marigolds, Coneflowers, Zinnias, Cosmos, Basil

### Seed Starting Timeline

| Weeks Before Last Frost | Start These |
|------------------------|-------------|
| 10-12 weeks | Peppers, Lavender, Foxglove |
| 8-10 weeks | Tomatoes, Snapdragons, Celosia, Strawflower |
| 6-8 weeks | Zinnias, Marigolds, Cosmos, Basil, most herbs |
| 4-6 weeks | Dahlia tubers, Lemongrass, Globe Amaranth |
| 2-4 weeks | Cucumbers, Melons, Squash (only if you want a head start) |

### Seeds That Need Special Treatment

| Seed | Treatment | Why |
|------|-----------|-----|
| Milkweed | Cold stratify 30 days (damp paper towel in fridge) | Mimics winter. Won't germinate without it |
| Goldenrod | Cold stratify 30 days | Same reason |
| Nasturtium | Soak 12-24 hours | Hard seed coat |
| Corn | Soak 8-12 hours | Speeds germination |
| Poppies | Do NOT bury. Scatter on surface of cold soil, press gently | Need light to germinate |
| Lavender | Cold stratify 2-4 weeks, then surface sow (needs light) | Notoriously difficult. Be patient |
| Eucalyptus | Cold stratify 4-6 weeks | Needs winter simulation |

## Hardening Off (The Step Everyone Skips, Then Wonders Why Their Plants Died)

Before moving seedlings from indoors to the garden, they need 7-10 days to adjust. Skip this and they will sunburn, wilt, or die.

| Day | Where to Put Them |
|-----|-------------------|
| Days 1-3 | Outside in shade, sheltered from wind. 2-3 hours only |
| Days 4-5 | 4-5 hours with some direct morning sun |
| Days 6-7 | 6+ hours of direct sun |
| Days 8-10 | Leave outside overnight (if no frost forecast) |

**If they wilt during hardening off:** Move back to shade, water, try again tomorrow. Better to slow down than to lose plants.

## Direct Sowing

### Soil Temperature Matters More Than Calendar Date

| Soil Temp | What You Can Sow |
|-----------|-----------------|
| 40-50F (early spring) | Poppies, Peas, Lettuce, Spinach, Kale, Carrots, Radishes, Milkweed, Goldenrod |
| 50-60F (mid-spring) | Cosmos, Nasturtiums, Bee Balm, Yarrow, Snapdragons, Herbs (most), Celery |
| 60-70F (late spring) | Zinnias, Sunflowers, Corn, Beans, Squash, Tomatoes, Peppers, Cucumbers, Melons |
| 70-80F (early summer) | Succession plantings of Zinnias/Sunflowers/Beans. Late corn. Late pumpkins |

**How to check soil temp:** Use a meat thermometer. Stick it 2" into soil at 8am. That's your baseline.

### Sowing Depth Rule of Thumb

Plant seeds **2-3x their diameter** deep. Tiny seeds (poppies, lettuce) go on the surface. Medium seeds (zinnias, cosmos) go 1/4" deep. Large seeds (corn, beans, sunflowers) go 1-2" deep.

### Wildflower Mix Sowing

1. Mix seeds 1:3 with sand or dry compost (prevents clumping)
2. Scatter over prepared soil (weeded, loosened top 1")
3. Press into soil with the flat of your hand or back of a rake
4. Do NOT bury deeply -- most wildflower seeds need light
5. Mist gently (don't blast with a hose)
6. Keep moist for 1-2 weeks until germination

## Transplanting Day

### The Day Before
- Water seedlings thoroughly
- Water the destination garden bed deeply

### Planting Steps
1. Dig hole 2x wider than the root ball, same depth
2. Gently remove plant from container (squeeze sides, tip upside down, catch the plant)
3. If roots are circling the bottom, gently tease them apart (root-bound plants don't spread out)
4. Place in hole at the SAME depth it was growing (deeper = stem rot, shallower = exposed roots)
5. Fill around with soil, press firmly to eliminate air pockets
6. Water deeply at the base immediately
7. Mulch 2-3" around the plant, leaving 1" gap at the stem

### Exceptions to Same-Depth Rule
- **Tomatoes:** Bury 2/3 of the stem. They grow roots along buried stems, making a stronger plant
- **Dahlias:** Plant tubers 4-6" deep, "eye" (growing point) facing up

### Best Time to Transplant
- **Cloudy days** or **late afternoon** (less transplant shock from direct sun)
- **Not** during a heat wave
- **Not** when soil is waterlogged

## Mulching (Do This for Every Bed)

| Material | Best For | Notes |
|----------|----------|-------|
| Shredded bark | Flower beds, perennials | Looks clean, decomposes slowly |
| Straw | Vegetable beds | Cheap, easy to work around. Avoid hay (full of weed seeds) |
| Pine straw/needles | Blueberries, acid-loving plants | Slightly acidifies soil |
| Shredded leaves | Everywhere | Free. Chop with mower first or they mat down |
| Cardboard | Under mulch, new beds | Smothers existing weeds. Will decompose in one season |

**Depth:** 2-3" for flower beds. 4-6" for winter protection.
**Gap:** Always leave 1" around plant stems. Mulch touching stems = rot.

## Companion Planting Quick Reference

| Plant This | Next To This | Why |
|-----------|-------------|-----|
| Nasturtiums | Tomatoes, Cucumbers, Squash | Trap aphids (they eat the nasturtium instead) |
| Marigolds | Tomatoes, Peppers, Beans | Deter nematodes and whiteflies |
| Basil | Tomatoes | Improves flavor, repels aphids and mosquitoes |
| Beans | Corn | Beans fix nitrogen; corn gives beans a trellis |
| Chives/Garlic | Roses, Raspberries | Deter Japanese beetles and aphids |
| Borage | Strawberries, Squash | Attracts pollinators, deters hornworms |
| Dill | Cabbage, Lettuce | Attracts beneficial wasps that eat caterpillars |

**Keep apart:**
- Fennel away from everything (it inhibits most plants)
- Potatoes away from tomatoes (share blight)
- Mint away from everything (it invades)

## Tools You Actually Need

| Tool | What For | Don't Bother With |
|------|----------|-------------------|
| Garden fork | Loosening soil, turning compost | Rototiller (destroys soil structure) |
| Hand trowel | Planting, digging small holes | Bulb planter (a trowel does the same thing) |
| Pruning shears | Deadheading, harvesting | Expensive Japanese scissors |
| Soaker hose | Watering beds efficiently | Sprinkler (wastes water, wets leaves) |
| Wheelbarrow | Moving soil, mulch, compost | Garden cart (wheelbarrow is more versatile) |
| 5-gallon buckets | Harvesting, mixing soil, carrying water | Anything labeled "garden caddy" |
| Kneeling pad | Your knees | Nothing -- just get one |
| Permanent marker + popsicle sticks | Labeling everything | Fancy plant markers (they all fade) |
