# Garden Care & Troubleshooting

> Weekly maintenance, common problems, quick fixes, and things that will ruin your garden if you ignore them.

---

## The Weekly Walk-Through (10 Minutes, Every Week, April-October)

Do this every week. It prevents 90% of garden disasters.

- [ ] **Water check:** Finger 1" into soil. Dry = water deeply. Moist = skip. Don't guess.
- [ ] **Deadhead:** Snip spent flowers at the stem base. This forces new blooms. Skip on plants you want to self-seed (Cosmos, Poppies).
- [ ] **Pest scan:** Look UNDER leaves for aphids. Check stems for powdery mildew. Check soil surface for slugs (look at dusk).
- [ ] **Weed pull:** Get them when they're small. 2 minutes now = 30-minute battle later.
- [ ] **Support check:** Tall plants leaning? Stake now. Dahlias, Sunflowers, Joe Pye Weed, Tomatoes -- they all need support before they fall.
- [ ] **Harvest check:** Pick ripe vegetables immediately. Leaving them signals the plant to stop producing.

## Watering Rules

| Rule | Why |
|------|-----|
| Water deeply, less often | Deep roots = strong plants. Shallow frequent watering = shallow roots that die in heat |
| Water in the morning | Leaves dry by afternoon, preventing fungal disease |
| Water the soil, not the leaves | Wet leaves = mildew, blight, and burned spots in sun |
| 1 inch per week | The general rule for most plants. Rain counts. |
| Container plants: daily in summer | Pots dry out 3-5x faster than ground beds |
| Cut back watering on ripening fruit | Excess water dilutes melon/tomato flavor and can split fruit |

**How to measure 1 inch:** Set an empty tuna can in the garden while watering. When it's full, you've hit 1 inch.

## Fertilizing

| When | What | For |
|------|------|-----|
| At planting | Compost mixed into soil | Everything |
| Every 2-4 weeks (growing season) | Balanced 10-10-10 | Heavy feeders: tomatoes, peppers, corn, sunflowers, dahlias |
| At flowering | Switch to low-nitrogen (5-10-10) | Tomatoes, peppers, melons |
| When fruit sets | Potassium boost (0-0-50) | Melons, squash |
| Never | High-nitrogen fertilizer | Nasturtiums, wildflowers (too much N = leaves, no flowers) |

**Caution:** Over-fertilizing is worse than under-fertilizing. Signs of too much nitrogen: lush dark green leaves, zero flowers, leggy growth. If this happens, stop feeding and let the plant catch up.

## Common Problems & Quick Fixes

### Leaves & Foliage

| Problem | What's Happening | Fix |
|---------|-----------------|-----|
| Yellow leaves (bottom up) | Nitrogen deficiency OR overwatering | Check soil moisture first. If wet, ease up. If dry, add compost |
| Yellow leaves (top/new growth) | Iron deficiency (common in high-pH soil) | Add garden sulfur or iron chelate |
| White powder on leaves | Powdery mildew (fungal) | Improve air circulation. Spray diluted neem oil or 1:10 milk-to-water solution |
| Black spots on leaves | Fungal leaf spot (from overhead watering) | Remove affected leaves. Water at soil level only |
| Holes in leaves | Caterpillars, beetles, or slugs | Hand-pick caterpillars. Neem oil for beetles. Beer traps for slugs |
| Leaves curling inward | Heat stress, aphids, or herbicide drift | Check for aphids underneath. If clean, increase water and mulch |
| Wilting in afternoon | Normal heat response OR underwatering | If recovered by morning, it's fine. If still wilted at sunrise, water immediately |
| Brown leaf edges | Salt buildup, underwatering, or wind burn | Flush soil with deep watering. Increase mulch |

### Pests

| Pest | Signs | Fix |
|------|-------|-----|
| Aphids (tiny green/black bugs) | Clusters on new growth, sticky residue | Blast with strong hose spray. Repeat 3 days in a row. Ladybugs eat them |
| Slugs | Holes in leaves, slime trails, damage appears overnight | Beer traps at dusk. Copper tape around raised beds. Hand-pick at night |
| Japanese beetles | Metallic green beetles, skeletonized leaves | Hand-pick into soapy water in the morning (they're sluggish). DO NOT use Japanese beetle traps -- they attract MORE beetles from the whole neighborhood |
| Squash vine borers | Sudden wilting of squash/pumpkin vine, sawdust-like frass at stem base | Slit the stem lengthwise with a razor, remove the borer, bury the stem in soil. Wrap base of stems with aluminum foil as prevention |
| Tomato hornworms | Large green caterpillar, stripped leaves | Hand-pick (they don't bite). If you see one covered in white rice-like cocoons, LEAVE IT -- those are parasitic wasp eggs that will kill future hornworms for you |
| Earwigs | Ragged holes in dahlia and zinnia petals, damage worst at night | Roll up newspaper, place near plants at dusk. Shake into soapy water in the morning |
| Deer | Plants eaten to stumps overnight | Fencing is the only reliable solution. Irish Spring soap bars and human hair are temporary deterrents at best |

### Diseases

| Disease | Signs | Fix |
|---------|-------|-----|
| Tomato blight (early) | Brown spots with concentric rings on lower leaves | Remove affected leaves. Mulch around base to prevent soil splash. Copper fungicide spray |
| Tomato blight (late) | Gray-green water-soaked spots, rapid plant death | Remove and destroy the plant (do NOT compost it). Fungal spores survive in soil. Rotate next year |
| Damping off (seedlings) | Seedlings fall over at soil line, stem pinched | Caused by too-wet soil and poor air flow. Use seed-starting mix (not garden soil). Bottom-water only. Increase air circulation |
| Root rot | Plant wilts even when soil is wet, roots are brown/mushy | Overwatering. Let soil dry out completely. Improve drainage. May not be salvageable |
| Powdery mildew | White powder on leaves (squash, cucumbers, zinnias are most susceptible) | Spray weekly with 1 part milk to 9 parts water. Improve spacing for air flow |

## Month-by-Month Maintenance

### Spring (March - May)
- **March:** Pull winter weeds before they seed. Top-dress beds with 2" compost. Check overwintered perennials -- cut back dead growth to 3" above soil
- **April:** Mulch everything 2-3" (leave 1" gap around stems). Set up soaker hoses. Begin hardening off indoor seedlings
- **May:** First round of deadheading. Stake tall plants NOW, not when they're already falling. Check newly transplanted seedlings daily

### Summer (June - August)
- **June:** Water consistently -- the #1 cause of garden failure is inconsistent watering in early summer. First pest wave hits now. Scout weekly
- **July:** Peak bloom for most flowers. Deep water in heat waves (morning only). Succession-sow zinnias and cosmos for fall blooms. Watch for powdery mildew as humidity rises
- **August:** Stop fertilizing perennials (they need to harden off before fall). Divide overgrown perennials. Start saving seeds from spent flowers. Second pest wave common

### Fall (September - November)
- **September:** Fall bloom season. Leave seed heads for birds. Stop deadheading -- let plants go to seed
- **October:** After first frost, cut dahlia stems to 4" and dig tubers for storage. Clean up spent annuals. Plant spring bulbs. Mulch perennials 4-6" for winter protection
- **November:** Final cleanup. Compost healthy plant waste. Trash (don't compost) anything that was diseased. Drain and store hoses. Clean and oil tools

### Winter (December - February)
- **December - January:** Order seeds. Review what worked and what failed. Update garden journal. Dream
- **February:** Start seeds indoors (6-8 weeks before last frost). Prep beds if soil is workable. Clean seed trays with 10% bleach solution before reuse

## Things That Will Ruin Your Garden (The "Don't" List)

1. **Don't skip hardening off seedlings.** Moving indoor seedlings straight outside kills them. It takes 7-10 days of gradual exposure. There is no shortcut
2. **Don't plant frost-tender plants before last frost.** Dahlias, tomatoes, peppers, melons, basil -- these die at 32F. One late frost wipes out weeks of work
3. **Don't water on a schedule.** Water when the soil needs it, not because it's Tuesday. Overwatering kills more plants than underwatering
4. **Don't ignore spacing.** "They're so small, I'll just tuck one more in" leads to mildew, leggy growth, and poor blooms by July
5. **Don't use treated lumber for vegetable beds.** Pressure-treated wood contains copper, arsenic, and/or chromium. Use untreated cedar, pine, or galvanized steel
6. **Don't compost diseased plants.** Home compost piles don't get hot enough to kill fungal spores. Bag and trash them
7. **Don't pull plants that look dead in early spring.** Many perennials (especially Bleeding Heart, Hosta, Dahlia) are late emergers. Wait until May before declaring anything dead
8. **Don't use landscape fabric under mulch in flower beds.** It works great for pathways but in planting beds it prevents self-seeding, traps moisture on stems, and becomes a weedy mess in 2 years. Use cardboard (it biodegrades) or just thick mulch
9. **Don't spray pesticides at midday.** Most pollinators are active 10am-4pm. If you must spray, do it at dusk when bees are in the hive
10. **Don't forget to enjoy it.** Walk through. Take pictures. Cut flowers for the table. This is the payoff

## Emergency Quick Reference

| Emergency | What to Do RIGHT NOW |
|-----------|---------------------|
| Late frost warning tonight | Cover tender plants with bedsheets, buckets, or frost cloth. Weight the edges. Remove covers by 10am |
| Hail damage | Don't prune damaged plants for 2 weeks. Let them recover. They often regrow from the crown |
| Plant completely wilted | Water deeply. If it doesn't recover in 2 hours, check roots for rot. If roots are white = it'll recover. Brown/mushy = remove |
| Whole bed of seedlings dead overnight | Cutworms. Dig around the dead stems -- you'll find a curled gray caterpillar. Put cardboard collars (toilet paper tubes) around remaining seedlings |
| Tomato plant suddenly wilts (one side or all) | Bacterial wilt or fusarium. Pull it immediately and trash it. Don't plant nightshades in that spot for 3 years |
| Unknown critter eating everything at night | Set up a phone camera on time-lapse. Usually deer, rabbits, or groundhogs. Solution depends on the culprit |

## Seed Saving (August – October)

Free seeds for next year from your own garden:

1. **Let some flowers go to seed** — don't deadhead everything
2. **Wait until seed heads are dry and brown** on the plant
3. **Cut the seed head and shake into a paper bag**
4. **Label and store** in a cool, dry place (envelope in a mason jar works)

**Easiest to save:** Zinnias, Cosmos, Marigolds, Sunflowers, Coneflowers
**Harder (but doable):** Chamomile, Poppies (tiny seeds)
**Don't bother saving:** Dahlias (propagate by dividing tubers in fall)
