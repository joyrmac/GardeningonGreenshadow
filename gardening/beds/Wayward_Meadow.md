| **Johnny Jump-Ups** (*Viola tricolor*) | Scattered throughout | Planted last year, now self-seeding. Purple blooms. Keep — they're beautiful and align with garden philosophy. |
| **Mock Strawberry** (*Potentilla indica*) | Patches in lawn | Serrated, dark green, creeping leaves. Produces yellow flowers and small inedible red berries. Prolific groundcover. |
| **Clover** | Mixed throughout | Fixing nitrogen for free. Don't spray. |
| **Lamb's Ear** (*Stachys byzantina*) | Established clumps | Available for division/transplanting to other beds (e.g., Edgeworth Corner). Medicinal — natural bandage plant. |
| **Dormant warm-season grass** (centipede/zoysia) | Underneath everything | Will green up in May and outcompete the winter weeds naturally. |

**Assessment:** The lawn doesn't need intervention. The warm-season grass will reclaim it by late spring. The "weeds" are protecting bare soil, feeding early pollinators, and several are edible/medicinal per the herbology table. Only pull weeds that are competing directly with planted beds.

## Concept

A naturalized prairie meadow — not a tended garden bed. The goal is a dense, self-sustaining mix of native grasses and wildflowers that supports pollinators, requires minimal maintenance after establishment, and looks intentionally wild (not neglected).

## Establishment Plan

### Year 1 (2026)
1. **Site prep:** Mow existing grass/weeds as short as possible. If heavy weeds, consider smothering with cardboard/newspaper for 4-6 weeks before planting
2. **Soil:** Prairie plants prefer lean soil — do NOT amend with compost or fertilizer. Rich soil favors weeds over native wildflowers
3. **Plant kits** according to kit instructions (likely scatter-seed or plug-plant)
4. **Fill gaps** with additional seeds from inventory list above
5. **Water** only during establishment (first 4-6 weeks if rain is scarce)
6. **Mow once** at end of first growing season (late fall) to 6-8" — this knocks back annual weeds and lets perennial roots establish

### Year 2+
- Mow or burn once annually in late winter/early spring (before new growth)
- Pull woody invasives if they appear
- Otherwise: leave it alone. That's the whole point

---

## Cautions

- **Don't mow during growing season** (April-October). The meadow will look "messy" in summer — that's correct. Mowing kills the wildflowers before they seed
- **Year 1 will look rough.** Most prairie perennials put energy into roots the first year, not flowers. You'll see mostly weeds and a few annuals. This is normal — the meadow fills in by year 2-3
- **Prairie plants don't need fertilizer.** Fertilizing encourages aggressive grasses and weeds that outcompete wildflowers
- **Expect some volunteer trees** (especially sweetgum, privet, or whatever's seeding from nearby). Pull these annually or they'll turn your meadow into scrub forest in 5 years
- **Milkweed needs cold stratification** before planting. If starting from seed, refrigerate in moist sand for 4-6 weeks, or direct-sow in late fall and let winter do the work
