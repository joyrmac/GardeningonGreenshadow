## The Vision

A shaded woodland room centered on the pergola swing, with fern groundcover replacing the ivy, surrounded by the existing mahonia, holly, and spruce as living walls. The bonfire pit stays as-is — a clearing in the woods. The flagstone path connects them.

**Mood:** Enclosed, cool, green, quiet. A place to sit on the swing with a book, or around the fire at night. Fernwood's sister garden.

## Reclamation Plan

### Phase 1: Ivy Removal (Priority — Do Now, March)

Late winter/early spring is ideal timing. Ground is soft, ivy is visible before other plants leaf out.

1. **Cut ivy at the base** of everything it's climbing — fence posts, holly trunk, mahonia stems, pergola posts. Sever all vines at ground level.
2. **Roll up ground ivy** in sections using a garden fork to loosen edges. The root system is shallow — it rolls up like a carpet. Work in sections.
3. **Bag and trash all ivy.** Do not compost — it re-roots from fragments.
4. **Wait 3-4 weeks** for aerial ivy (on fence, holly, etc.) to die and brown out. Then peel it off — it comes off much easier once dead.
5. **Monitor for regrowth** every 2-3 weeks through spring. Pull new shoots while small. By midsummer the battle is mostly won.

### Phase 2: Pergola & Path (After Ivy Is Cleared)

1. **Hard-prune the pergola vine** back to 2-3 main structural stems. Late winter (now) is perfect timing. It will regrow and cover the roof by summer in a controlled way.
2. **Remove dead vine mass** from fence area.
3. **Inspect pergola wood** for rot — check joints, posts, and lattice panels. Replace lattice if needed.
4. **Clear the flagstone path** — sweep leaf litter, remove branch pile. Power-wash moss if desired (or leave it — moss is character).

### Phase 3: Fern Groundcover (Spring, After Ivy Is Gone)

Replace the ivy carpet with a mix of shade-loving ferns. The area is shaded, the soil is acidic from oak/pine leaf litter, and ferns will create the lush woodland floor this space wants.

**Planting plan:**

| Fern | Role | Qty | Notes |
|------|------|-----|-------|
| **Christmas Fern** (*Polystichum acrostichoides*) | Evergreen base layer | 8-12 | Native. Stays green all winter so the space never looks bare. The backbone. |
| **Hay-Scented Fern** (*Dennstaedtia punctilobula*) | Spreading groundcover | 4-6 | Native. Spreads by runners like ivy did — fills ground fast. Smells sweet when brushed. |
| **Southern Shield Fern** (*Thelypteris kunthii*) | Spreading groundcover (alternative) | 4-6 | Native. Another aggressive spreader option if hay-scented isn't available. |
| **Japanese Painted Fern** (*Athyrium niponicum*) | Showpiece accent | 3-4 | Silver-purple fronds. Plant near the swing and along the flagstone path where you'll see them up close. |
| **Autumn Fern** (*Dryopteris erythrosora*) | Color accent | 2-3 | Semi-evergreen. Copper-red new fronds in spring for seasonal color. |

**Placement:**
- Christmas fern as the general carpet — scatter throughout the cleared areas
- Hay-scented or southern shield in the open ground between the bonfire pit and pergola
- Japanese painted fern flanking the flagstone path and near the swing seat
- Autumn fern near the mahonia and holly bases for the spring copper flush
- Let oak leaf litter remain as natural mulch between ferns — don't rake

**Sourcing:** Check Prairie Moon Nursery (native ferns), local NC nurseries, or divide from existing ferns if any are on the property. The Fernwood plan already includes Christmas fern, maidenhair, lady fern, and cinnamon fern orders from Breck's — coordinate to avoid duplicate purchases.

## What NOT to Do

- **Don't remove the mahonia.** It's a dramatic structural plant that blooms in winter when nothing else does. Perfect for this space.
- **Don't remove the holly.** It's a major bird food source and provides year-round screening.
- **Don't remove the pergola vine entirely.** Once pruned back to 2-3 stems, it will provide shade over the swing in summer. That's the whole point of a pergola vine.
- **Don't pave or hardscape the bonfire area.** Bare ground/natural clearing is fine. Fire + pavers = cracked pavers.
- **Don't plant anything flammable near the bonfire pit.** Keep fern plantings at least 8-10 ft from the fire area.

---

## Cautions

- **Mahonia has spiny leaves** — wear gloves when working around it. The berries are technically edible but taste terrible raw (they make decent jelly).
- **Holly berries are mildly toxic** to humans (cause vomiting). Keep kids from eating them. Birds are fine.
- **Ferns need consistent moisture to establish.** Water newly planted ferns regularly for the first 4-6 weeks. After that, the shade and leaf litter mulch should keep them happy.
- **Don't fertilize ferns heavily.** They prefer lean, acidic, woodsy soil — which is exactly what this area already has.
- **Check the pergola vine ID** when it leafs out. If it's wisteria, it needs aggressive annual pruning or it'll take over again. If it's trumpet vine, same deal. Either way, prune hard once a year after blooming.

## Bonfire Pit Notes

- Current setup: bare ground clearing with ash pile, picnic table
- The ash from hardwood fires is good — scatter it thinly in the garden beds as a potassium/calcium amendment (not near acid-loving plants like blueberries)
- Keep a 10 ft clearance from the pergola and fence when burning
- The cleared area doesn't need planting — it's functional space
