# Obelisk Garden Plan
## Tomato & Pepper Circle with Vertical Structures

> **Location:** The Gloam / Tomato Circle
> **Size:** Circular arrangement with 4-6 obelisks
> **Climate:** Zone 7b/8a (NC)
> **Last frost date:** April 15 (conservative)
> **Season:** May-October

---

## Philosophy

**Vertical = More Space.** Obelisks maximize growing area, improve air circulation (reduce disease), and create visual drama. This isn't just functional — it's beautiful, witchy, and productive.

**Companion Planting = Natural Pest Control.** Marigolds repel nematodes. Basil protects tomatoes. Borage attracts pollinators and deters hornworms. Nasturtiums trap pests. No synthetic chemicals needed.

**Succession Planting = Continuous Harvest.** Plant determinates and indeterminates in waves, so while early tomatoes fade, mid-season ones peak, and late varieties mature into fall.

## Planting Timeline

### **Phase 1: INDOORS (March 1-15)**
- [ ] Start tomato seeds indoors (8-10 weeks before last frost = ~March 1)
  - Sungold, Cherokee Purple, San Marzano, Mountain Magic
  - Use seed-starting mix, fluorescent lights, warm mat (70°F)
- [ ] Start pepper seeds indoors (same time — they're slow)
  - Bell or Thai Hot
- [ ] **BUY these as transplants if you don't want to start from seed:**
  - Basil varieties (can't start too early — frost risk)
  - Nasturtium (direct sow later)
  - Borage (direct sow or buy transplants)

**Task Checklist:**
- [ ] Gather containers, seed-starting mix, labels, fluorescent lights
- [ ] Start seeds (16 cells minimum: 4 tomato varieties × 2-3 each, 2-3 pepper cells)
- [ ] Mist soil until germination (70°F, keep moist but not soggy)
- [ ] Provide lights 14-16 hours/day (germination to true leaves)

---

### **Phase 2: HARDENING OFF (April 15-May 1)**

**Last frost date: April 15 (conservative) to May 1 (actual)**

- [ ] Begin hardening off transplants on April 15
  - Week 1: 1-2 hours sun per day (shaded)
  - Week 2: 3-4 hours sun per day (dappled)
  - Week 3: Full day in sun, bring in at night
  - Week 4: Leave out overnight (if temps >50°F)
- [ ] **BUY basil, nasturtium, borage transplants** (if not growing from seed)
- [ ] Prepare garden beds
  - Clear weeds/old plants
  - Add 3-4" compost, mix in
  - Install obelisks (sunk 12" deep for stability)

**Task Checklist:**
- [ ] Set up hardening-off station (cold frame or sheltered patio)
- [ ] Inspect transplants for leggy growth (pinch if needed)
- [ ] Prepare soil in obelisk circle (amend with compost/worm castings)
- [ ] Buy stakes, ties, fertilizer if needed

---

### **Phase 3: TRANSPLANT OUTDOORS (May 1-15)**

**Plant after last frost (May 1) when soil is 60°F+**

**Obelisk Tomatoes (May 1-10):**
- [ ] Harden off indoor tomato starts (see phase 2)
- [ ] Plant 1 tomato per obelisk into garden
  - Bury deep (up to first true leaves — encourages root growth)
  - Spacing: 24-30" from obelisk center
  - Add handful of compost to planting hole
  - Water immediately
- [ ] Install tomato cages or spiral supports (obelisk will be trellis)

**Peppers (May 5-15):**
- [ ] Harden off indoor pepper starts
- [ ] Plant 1-2 peppers at obelisk 5 base
  - Spacing: 18-24" from center
  - Plant in warm soil (peppers hate cold!)
  - Add compost to hole
  - Water well

**Direct Sow (May 10-20):**
- [ ] **Nasturtium** (frost-sensitive, direct sow)
  - 3-5 seeds in clusters
  - Plant edge of garden (downwind from tomatoes if possible)
  - Spacing: 12" apart

**Buy & Transplant (May 5-15):**
- [ ] **Basil transplants** (6-10 plants)
  - 2-3 around each tomato obelisk base
  - Spacing: 8-12" apart
  - Water immediately (tender plants)

- [ ] **Marigold transplants** (20-30 plants)
  - Plant in rings around obelisks
  - Outer ring: 18-24" from center
  - Inner ring: 12-15" from base
  - Spacing: 10-12" apart
  - Water in well

- [ ] **Borage plants** (2-3 transplants)
  - Scatter around perimeter
  - Spacing: 18" apart

**Task Checklist:**
- [ ] Plant obelisk tomatoes (May 1-10)
- [ ] Plant peppers (May 5-15)
- [ ] Direct sow nasturtium (May 10)
- [ ] Transplant basil, marigolds, borage (May 5-15)
- [ ] Install mulch around plants (2-3" depth, 1" from crowns)
- [ ] Set up watering system (drip preferred, or soaker hose)
- [ ] Install support stakes for basil if needed (tall varieties)

---

### **Phase 4: EARLY SEASON CARE (May-June)**

**Weekly Tasks:**
- [ ] Water deeply 2-3x per week (1-1.5" total)
  - Soil-level watering (avoid foliage — disease prevention)
  - Early morning best
- [ ] Pinch off lower tomato leaves (12" from ground) — improves air flow
- [ ] Pinch basil tops (encourage bushiness, delay flowering)
- [ ] Deadhead marigolds (promotes continuous bloom)
- [ ] Watch for pests (aphids, spider mites, whiteflies)
  - Strong water spray can dislodge aphids
  - Insecticidal soap if needed (neem oil not on flowers in heat)

**Every 2 Weeks:**
- [ ] Fertilize with balanced 10-10-10 or tomato-specific formula
  - Follow package directions
  - Water before fertilizing

**As Needed:**
- [ ] Remove bottom tomato suckers (keep 1-2 main stems on indeterminates)
- [ ] Tie up tomato vines to obelisk as they grow
- [ ] Remove any diseased leaves (early blight prevention)

**By Early June:**
- Tomatoes flowering, first flowers visible
- Basil ready for first pinches/harvest
- Marigolds blooming heavily
- Nasturtium flowering (may have aphids — that's OK!)

---

### **Phase 5: PEAK SEASON (June-August)**

**Daily Check-In (5-10 min):**
- [ ] Water (1.5-2" per week in peak heat)
- [ ] Look for ripe tomatoes (begin harvest mid-June)
- [ ] Deadhead marigolds
- [ ] Pinch basil leaves for kitchen use

**Weekly (30-45 min):**
- [ ] Fertilize (every 7-10 days in heat)
- [ ] Tie up new tomato growth to obelisk
- [ ] Inspect for pests (spider mites, hornworms, rot)
  - Remove infected fruit immediately
  - Hand-pick hornworms if visible
  - Hose plants if spider mites present (mid-morning)
- [ ] Harvest ripe fruit
  - Tomatoes: fully colored, slight give when squeezed
  - Peppers: can pick green or fully colored (red=sweeter)
  - Basil: pinch leaves from top (encourages bushiness)

**Pest Management:**
| Pest | Sign | Fix |
|------|------|-----|
| **Aphids** | Curled leaves, sticky residue, on nasturtium | Let nasturtium suffer (trap crop). Strong water spray. Insecticidal soap if severe. |
| **Spider Mites** | Fine webbing, mottled leaves (red/yellow) | Hose plants mid-morning. Repeat daily if needed. Neem oil as backup. |
| **Hornworms** | Large holes in leaves, dark droppings | Hand-pick (watch for parasitic wasp cocoons — leave them!). Plant borage for wasp habitat. |
| **Early Blight** | Brown spots on lower leaves | Remove leaves immediately. Improve air flow. Water soil-level only (never foliage). |
| **Septoria Leaf Spot** | Small circular spots with gray centers | Remove affected leaves. Same prevention as early blight. |
| **Powdery Mildew** | White powder on leaves (rare on tomatoes) | Thin plants for air flow. Sulfur dust if severe. |

### **Phase 7: FALL & SEASON'S END (September-October)**

**As Weather Cools (Late September):**
- [ ] Frost watch begins (first freeze typically Oct 15-Nov 1)
- [ ] Harvest all mature fruit before frost
- [ ] Let peppers fully color (more heat + flavor)
- [ ] Bring green tomatoes indoors to ripen (if frost threatens)
- [ ] Final basil harvest (before frost kills it)

**Cleanup (After Frost):**
- [ ] Remove tomato vines (compost if disease-free; otherwise trash)
- [ ] Pull basil plants (compost)
- [ ] Pull marigolds, nasturtium (compost — unless seeded for next year)
- [ ] Preserve peppers (dry, freeze, or pickle hot varieties)
- [ ] Leave borage if self-seeding (may come back year 2)

**Soil Building:**
- [ ] Spread compost 2-3" deep over entire bed
- [ ] Turn under gently (don't bury deep)
- [ ] Plant cover crop (clover, rye, buckwheat) if space allows
- [ ] Let bed rest over winter

## Watering Strategy

**Goal:** Consistent moisture (1.5-2" per week), soil-level only

**Best Method:** Soaker hose or drip irrigation
- Lay soaker hose in loops around obelisks
- Water 45-60 min per day (early morning, 6-8 am)
- OR 2-3 deep soakings per week instead

**Manual Watering (if no drip system):**
- Bucket or wand at soil level (never overhead — disease risk)
- 1.5-2" per week = ~1 gallon per plant per watering (adjust for rain)
- Water early morning (best) or evening (acceptable)
- Avoid wet foliage (early blight, septoria, powdery mildew)

**Rain Gauge Tip:**
Set a shallow container in garden. If it collects 1/2", you've watered enough that day. If rain gives you 1.5-2", skip watering.

**Heat Stress Signs:**
- Wilting (especially afternoon) = urgent watering needed
- Sunscald on fruit = mulch/shade cloth
- Blossom end rot (dark spot on bottom of fruit) = inconsistent watering (calcium deficiency trigger)

## Fertilizing Strategy

**Timeline:**
- **First fertilizer:** At planting (compost in hole = enough for first month)
- **Ongoing:** Every 7-10 days starting mid-June (peak growth + heat)
- **Switch to potassium-heavy (low N) at fruit set** = encourages ripening instead of leaves

**Formulas:**
- **April-May:** 10-10-10 balanced (growth phase)
- **June-July:** 5-10-10 or tomato-specific (fruit set phase)
- **August-Sept:** 5-10-15 or "finish" formula (ripening phase)

**Application:**
- Follow package directions (usually 1 tbsp per gallon, or granular scatter)
- Water before fertilizing (prevents burn)
- Apply soil-level, not on foliage
- Organic options: compost tea, fish emulsion, seaweed extract (all good!)

**Signs of Nutrient Problems:**
| Sign | Problem | Fix |
|------|---------|-----|
| Yellow lower leaves, veins green | Nitrogen deficiency | Increase fertilizer strength |
| Blossom end rot (dark spot on fruit bottom) | Calcium/water inconsistency | Mulch better, water consistently |
| Purple/bronze leaves | Phosphorus deficiency (cold) | Fertilize with higher P (unlikely in heat) |
| Pale new leaves | Iron deficiency | Unlikely in NC soil — check pH |

## Pest & Disease Prevention Checklist

### **PREVENTION (Most Important)**
- [ ] **Air flow:** Space plants properly (24-30" tomatoes, 10-12" marigolds)
- [ ] **Mulch:** 2-3" around base, 1" away from stems
- [ ] **Soil-level water:** Never wet foliage (early blight trigger)
- [ ] **Companion planting:** Basil + marigolds + borage already on job
- [ ] **Remove lower leaves:** Pinch bottom 12" of tomato foliage (air flow + disease prevention)
- [ ] **Stake/support:** Keeps fruit off soil (prevents rot)

### **MONITORING (Weekly Check-In)**
- [ ] Walk through garden, inspect leaf tops AND undersides
- [ ] Look for holes, discoloration, sticky residue, webbing
- [ ] Check fruit for spots, rot, cracks
- [ ] Note any pest sightings (hornworms, beetles, aphids on nasturtium)

### **TREATMENT (If Problems Arise)**
- **Organic first:** Strong water spray, hand-pick, insecticidal soap
- **Neem oil:** Effective but avoid during peak heat, flowers, and evening (burns foliage)
- **Sulfur dust:** For powdery mildew (rare on tomatoes)
- **Remove affected leaves immediately:** Don't compost diseased material
- **Trash bad fruit:** Don't let rot spread

## Harvesting & Storage

### **Tomatoes**
- **Ripeness:** Full color (variety-specific), slight give when squeezed, fragrant
- **Frequency:** Every 2-3 days (don't let overripe fruit split/attract pests)
- **Time:** Early morning (firmest)
- **Storage:** Room temperature on counter (70-75°F best). NOT in fridge (loses flavor + texture)
- **Life:** 3-7 days at room temperature depending on ripeness
- **Green tomatoes:** Pick at "breaker stage" (first hint of color), ripen indoors on windowsill

### **Basil**
- **Pick:** Leaves & small stems from top (encourages bushiness)
- **Frequency:** Every 1-2 weeks (or continuous pinches)
- **Storage (fresh):** In water like cut flowers (lasts 5-7 days). NOT in fridge (bruises)
- **Storage (dried):** Hang bundles upside-down in warm, dry spot. Crumble into jars. Lasts 3-6 months
- **Pesto:** Blend fresh basil + garlic + nuts + cheese + oil. Freeze in ice cube trays

### **Peppers**
- **Ripeness:** Full size, firm, glossy. Can pick green or wait for color change (red = sweeter, spicier for hot types)
- **Frequency:** Every few days (or leave for full color)
- **Storage:** Plastic bag in crisper drawer, 1-2 weeks
- **Drying (hot peppers):** Hang strings of peppers to dry (2-4 weeks). Store in jars. Grind into flakes or powder

### **Marigolds**
- **Edible petals:** Pinch just the petals (not the bitter green base). Add to salads (peppery flavor)
- **Deadheading:** Remove whole flowers before seed formation (forces more blooms)
- **Seed saving:** Let 2-3 flowers go to seed. Dry on plant. Collect seed heads in fall. Store dry seeds for next year

## Notes & Tips

### **Obelisk Stability**
- Sink obelisks 12-18" deep into ground
- Anchor in compost-amended soil (not clay)
- Test stability by leaning (should barely move)
- Stake to fence/post if in windy area

### **Companion Planting Magic**
- **Basil + Tomato:** Repels thrips, whiteflies. Magical pairing.
- **Marigold + Everything:** Nematode control. Pest deterrent. Edible beauty.
- **Nasturtium:** Sacrifice plant — let it get aphids so tomatoes don't.
- **Borage:** Pollinator magnet. Deters hornworms. Self-seeds.

### **Succession Planting**
- Plant determinate (Mountain Magic) early → harvest June-July → frees space for late plantings
- Indeterminate varieties (Sungold, Cherokee, San Marzano) keep producing all summer
- Peppers slow to mature — give them full season (don't cut short)

### **Witchy Garden Touches**
- Install "Tomato Circle" or "The Gloam" sign at entrance
- Arrange obelisks in circle or spiral (intentional geometry = witchy)
- Lay crystals around base of sign (clear quartz, rose quartz, citrine)
- Hang string lights above obelisks for evening magic
- Plant by lunar phases if you're into it (waxing moon for growth, full moon for peak harvest)

### **Seed Saving (Year 2+)**
- Let 1-2 best tomato plants go to fruit — collect seed from ripest tomatoes
- Ferment seeds in water 2-3 days, rinse, dry on paper
- Marigolds: Let seed heads dry on plant, collect in fall
- Basil: Let one plant flower, collect dried seed heads
- Hot peppers: Let ripen fully, dry whole, save seed from best fruits

### **Flavor Notes by Variety**
- **Sungold:** Sweet, fruity, cherry bomb — eat right off vine
- **Cherokee Purple:** Deep, complex, smoky beefsteak — slice thick for salads
- **San Marzano:** Low acid, dense, sweet — perfect for sauce/passata
- **Mountain Magic:** Reliable, classic tomato taste — good all-arounder
- **Pepper (Bell):** Sweet, crisp when cooked — stuff or roast
- **Pepper (Thai Hot):** Extreme heat — dry for flakes or make hot sauce

## Success Checklist (Print & Tape to Garden Shed!)

**BEFORE PLANTING (April):**
- [ ] Prepare obelisk bed (clear, amend, level)
- [ ] Install obelisks (secure, 12-18" deep)
- [ ] Start seeds indoors OR buy transplants
- [ ] Buy supporting plants (basil, marigolds, etc.)

**AT PLANTING (May 1-15):**
- [ ] Harden off transplants
- [ ] Plant obelisk tomatoes (bury deep)
- [ ] Plant obelisk peppers
- [ ] Direct sow nasturtium
- [ ] Transplant basil, marigolds, borage
- [ ] Apply mulch (2-3" depth)
- [ ] Install watering system

**EARLY SEASON (May-June):**
- [ ] Water 2-3x per week (soil level)
- [ ] Fertilize every 7-10 days
- [ ] Pinch basil (encourage bushiness)
- [ ] Remove lower tomato leaves (air flow)
- [ ] Deadhead marigolds
- [ ] Watch for early pests

**PEAK SEASON (July-September):**
- [ ] Harvest daily (tomatoes every 2-3 days)
- [ ] Tie up tomato growth as it climbs obelisk
- [ ] Fertilize weekly
- [ ] Monitor for pests (spider mites, hornworms)
- [ ] Deadhead spent flowers
- [ ] Keep basil pinched
- [ ] Journal ripening dates & flavor notes

**SEASON'S END (September-October):**
- [ ] Harvest before frost
- [ ] Preserve excess (sauce, drying, freezing)
- [ ] Collect seed from best plants
- [ ] Remove vines (compost or trash)
- [ ] Add compost to bed for next year
