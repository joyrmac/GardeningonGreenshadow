## Cat Garden

**Location:** East side of property, near house
**Shape:** U-shaped bed formed by 3 railroad ties, opening west toward the house
**Sun:** Partial shade — mature tulip magnolia (8ft+) in the center blocks most direct sun
**Cats:** 3 outdoor cats

### What's There Now (March 2026)
- Catnip and catmint planted
- Sand ground cover
- Tulip magnolia dominates the center
- Railroad ties form the structure

### The Problem
The garden is bare except for catnip, catmint, and sand. The magnolia creates heavy shade across most of the bed, so sun-loving plants need to go at the open west end. Three cats need more variety to nibble, roll in, and lounge around.

**Under the Magnolia (full shade — lounging zone):**
No plants here — the magnolia canopy is too dense. This is cat territory.

- Flat sunbathing rocks (place at the west edge where dappled light reaches)
- Pine bark mulch or plain dirt — **NO cedar, it's toxic to cats**
- 1-2 small logs for scratching, hiding, perching
- Leave some bare dirt — cats like to roll in it

### What NOT to Plant
- **NO lilies of any kind** — extremely toxic to cats, even the pollen
- **NO cedar mulch** — cedar oil is toxic to cats
- **Skip valerian** — attracts rats, and with 3 cats already using the garden, you don't need another cat attractant
- **NO lemongrass in the shady area** — it needs full sun and won't survive under the magnolia

### What to Buy
- Cat grass seed (oat grass or wheatgrass) — ~$3-5 for a big bag
- A few flat rocks for the lounging zone (check landscape supply or use what's on property)
- Pine bark mulch if not already on hand
- Everything else is already in your seed inventory

### Realistic Expectations
The magnolia is the star here — it's not going away and it defines the space. Lean into it. The cats probably already lounge under it. The goal is to make the west opening a cat buffet of things to nibble and roll in, keep the shady center comfortable for lounging, and let the railroad tie edges have some color. Don't fight the shade — work with it.

### Action Plan — Cat Garden

**Phase 1: Prep (1-2 hours)**
1. Check the catnip and catmint — are they alive and growing, or did they die back? If alive, note where they are so you don't disturb them
2. If the catnip is NOT already in a sunken pot, dig it up, put it in a plastic pot with the bottom cut out, and re-sink it. It WILL take over otherwise
3. Rake the sand patch smooth — decide if you want to keep it (cats like rolling in sand) or replace with mulch
4. Place flat rocks at the west edge of the magnolia canopy where dappled sun hits — this is the sunbathing spot
5. Add 1-2 small logs under the magnolia for hiding/perching
6. Top dress bare soil under the magnolia with pine bark mulch (NOT cedar). Leave some bare dirt patches — cats prefer them

**Phase 2: Plant the west opening (30 min)**
All of these go in the open west end where sunlight enters:
1. Direct sow cat grass (oat or wheatgrass) in a 1-2 ft patch — scratch into soil, water. Resow every 3-4 weeks for fresh supply
2. Direct sow chamomile (Roman) seed around the cat grass — scatter lightly, press into soil, don't bury deep
3. Direct sow creeping thyme between the chamomile and the railroad tie edges — it'll fill in as living ground cover
4. Water everything gently

**Phase 3: Plant the tie edges (20 min)**
1. Sow bunny tails grass along the south railroad tie (best light)
2. Sow nasturtium (Alaska Mix) at the west corners of each tie where sun reaches
3. Sow calendula mixed with the nasturtiums
4. Water

**Phase 4: Ongoing**
- Resow cat grass every 3-4 weeks (cats will mow it down)
- Let chamomile, thyme, and nasturtiums self-seed — they'll fill in more each year
- Pine bark mulch annually under the magnolia
- Pull anything that's not cat-safe if volunteer plants show up

## Porch Planters (3 Large Pots)

**Pot 1 -- Full Sun:**
- Center: Purple Coneflower (Echinacea)
- Off-center: Black-Eyed Susan (Rudbeckia)
- Back/side: Little Bluestem or ornamental grass

**Pot 2 -- Part Sun / Bright Shade:**
- Clustered: 2 Coral Bells (Heuchera)
- Edge/back: Carex (sedge) or small fern

**Pot 3 -- Herb/Scent Pot:**
- Center: Lavender (English)
- Edge: Thyme or Oregano (trailing)
- Opposite: Lemon Balm OR Mint (never both, and only one -- it takes over)

**Soil refresh:** Replace top 4-6" of old soil with 70% existing + 20-25% compost + 5-10% worm castings. Top with bark mulch.

**Caution:** Container plants dry out MUCH faster than in-ground plants. In summer, check daily. In a heatwave, you may need to water twice a day. If leaves wilt by midday but recover by evening, you're fine. If they're still wilted at sunset, water immediately.

## Blueberry & Raspberry Bed

**Dimensions:** ~35 ft long. East to west orientation.
**What's actually here (March 2026):** 4 blueberry bushes. Mimosa tree removed. No sunflowers, no Boston Ivy (original plan scrapped).

### Current Layout: Blueberry Row

4 bushes, 4-5 ft apart, ripening in sequence:
1. O'Neal (late May) -- SW corner
2. Patriot (early June)
3. Bluecrop (mid-June)
4. Legacy (late July) -- SE corner

### New: Raspberry Row (parallel, south side)

4 Red Latham canes transplanted from the old 2x3 brick box into a parallel row on the south side of the blueberries, with a 3-4 ft picking aisle between them.

```
Blueberry row:  O'Neal --- Patriot --- Bluecrop --- Legacy
                         (4-5 ft apart)
— 3-4 ft picking aisle (mulch path or mow strip) —
Raspberry row:  cane --- cane --- cane --- cane
                      (4-5 ft apart, wire trellis)
```

**Why this works:** Both crops want acidic soil (pH 5.5-6.5 for raspberries, 4.5-5.5 for blueberries). Same amendments (peat moss, pine fines, elemental sulfur), same watering schedule. The picking aisle keeps them from tangling and gives airflow — critical for both crops in humid NC.

### Raspberry Trellis

Two options depending on what you have:

**Option A — brush pile stakes + wire (free):**
- Drive 2 sturdy forked posts (from brush pile, NE of Wildwood) at each end of the row, 6-8" deep
- Run galvanized wire or heavy jute twine at 2 ft and 4 ft heights
- Tie canes loosely to wires as they grow

**Option B — T-post + wire (buy, more permanent):**
- 2 metal T-posts at each end ($4-6 each at Home Depot)
- 14-gauge galvanized wire at 2 ft and 4 ft
- Lasts years with no maintenance

### Materials (recycled from property)

| Need | Source | Notes |
|------|--------|-------|
| Planting soil | **Dirt pile** + brush pile bottom compost | Mix 50/50, add peat moss or pine fines to acidify |
| Mulch (4-6") | **Brush pile** — decomposed leaves and bark from bottom | Keep 6" clear of cane stems |
| Trellis posts | **Brush pile** (forked hardwood) or buy 2 T-posts | See trellis options above |
| Root barrier | **Buy plastic edging, 12" deep** | Sink along outer (south) edge of raspberry row |

### Build Steps

1. Dig up 4 Red Latham canes from current location. Keep root balls intact, wrap in damp burlap or bag
2. Mark the raspberry row 3-4 ft south of the blueberry row, parallel to it
3. Dig 4 holes, 4-5 ft apart, each 18" wide x 12" deep
4. Mix planting soil (dirt pile + brush pile compost + peat moss). Fill holes halfway
5. Plant canes at same depth they were growing. Backfill, firm gently, water deeply
6. Set trellis posts at each end. Run wire or twine at 2 ft and 4 ft
7. Sink root barrier 12" deep along the south (outer) edge of the row
8. Mulch 4-6" thick — keep 6" clear of stems to prevent fungal issues
9. Mulch or mow the 3-4 ft aisle between rows
10. Water deeply again

**Do NOT plant near berries:**

| Plant | Why |
|-------|-----|
| Fennel | Inhibits growth of most nearby plants (allelopathic) |
| Mint (uncontained) | Will outcompete everything via runners — only in buried pots |
| Tomatoes/peppers/eggplant | Share verticillium wilt with raspberries |
| Blackberries | Disease sharing (raspberry mosaic virus) |

**Cautions:**
- **Both crops need acidic soil.** Amend with peat moss, pine fines, or elemental sulfur. Test pH annually — NC clay drifts alkaline
- **Raspberries spread by underground suckers** 6+ feet out. Root barrier on the south edge and pulling escapees annually handles this
- **Don't plant raspberries near blackberries** — shared diseases (raspberry mosaic virus, verticillium wilt)
- **Prune correctly:** Red Latham is summer-bearing — fruits on 2nd-year canes. After harvest, cut fruited canes to the ground. Leave new green canes for next year
- **Don't fertilize raspberries at transplant.** Let them establish 3-4 weeks first
- **Bird netting** is essential for blueberries unless you want to share your entire crop with mockingbirds. Raspberries attract birds too — consider netting both rows once fruit sets
- **Don't use moldy brush pile material** right against cane stems. Use the cleanest decomposed material closest to plants

### Mailbox Garden (Pollinator Cottage Garden)

**Location:** Cul-de-sac street edge, right side of driveway. High visibility from the road — this will be the first thing people see.

**Vibe:** Wild pollinator-native cottage garden with herbs woven in. Not manicured — overflowing, buzzy, a little chaotic. The kind of mailbox garden people slow down to look at.

**Size:** Roughly 8-10 ft wide once hollies are removed. The existing mulched bed gives the footprint.

**Sun:** West-facing, full brutal afternoon sun. Everything here must handle heat.

**Plan:** Remove holly and rose. Plant in drifts, not rows. Let things self-seed and get wilder each year.

**What to buy:**

- Possibly 1 lavender plant for the same reason

**Holly + rose removal:**
These are BIG established hollies — this is a real job, not a quick pull.
1. Cut hollies back to stumps with loppers/saw first to clear the foliage
2. Dig around the root ball (expect 18-24" wide roots on bushes this size)
3. Pry out with a digging bar or mattock. May need a truck and chain for the largest one
4. Wear heavy gloves — holly leaves are brutal
5. Dig out rose bush separately. If it's grafted, the rootstock can resprout — get the whole root
6. Remove old mulch and amend the soil with compost — holly acidifies the soil over time, and the new plants prefer neutral to slightly acidic
7. Consider renting a stump grinder if the holly stumps are too big to pull

**Caution:** West-facing beds get brutal afternoon sun. Even "full sun" plants can struggle in July/August heat. Mulch heavily (3-4"), water deeply in the morning, and expect some mid-summer wilt on the hottest days. This is normal -- the plants aren't dying, they're conserving water.

### Action Plan — Mailbox Garden

**Phase 1: Demolition (half day, hard work)**
1. Cut all holly bushes to stumps with loppers or a reciprocating saw
2. Bag or haul away the cut branches (holly doesn't compost well — too waxy and spiny)
3. Dig around each stump. Expect 18-24" root balls on bushes this mature
4. Pry out with a digging bar, mattock, or truck+chain. Don't leave the roots — they can resprout
5. Dig out the rose bush. Get the whole root — grafted roses resprout from rootstock
6. Rake out old mulch and any holly leaf debris
7. Consider renting a stump grinder if any stump is too big to pull (~$150/half day rental)

**Phase 2: Soil prep (1-2 hours)**
1. Amend the bed with 2-3 bags of compost — holly acidifies soil and the holes will be compacted clay
2. Turn the compost into the top 8-10" of existing soil
3. Rake level
4. Don't add fertilizer yet — let the live plants establish first

**Phase 3: Plant the live plants (1 hour)**
These are 3" pots — small but they'll fill in by summer. Space them out across the bed:

```
ROAD / CURB EDGE
  ·  ·  ·  ·  ·  ·  ·  ·  ·  ·

  Coreopsis    Salvia    [MAILBOX]    Salvia-area     Coreopsis-area
  (front)      (front)               seeds here       seeds here

      Hyssop       Echinacea       BES
      (middle)     (middle)        (middle)

          (back — seeds fill in here)
  ·  ·  ·  ·  ·  ·  ·  ·  ·  ·
TOWARD HOUSE / DRIVEWAY
```

1. **Back (farthest from road):** Plant Magnus Echinacea and Goldsturm Black Eyed Susan — these get tallest (2-3 ft)
2. **Middle:** Plant Mango Tango Hyssop between the two back plants — it'll fill the gap with orange
3. **Front-middle (beside mailbox):** Plant May Night Salvia on one side, Early Sunrise Coreopsis on the other — both are compact (18")
4. Water all 5 deeply after planting. Mulch around them 2-3"

**Phase 4: Seed the gaps (30 min)**
After the live plants are in, scatter seeds in the open spaces between them:
1. **Behind the live plants (back edge):** Scatter cosmos (Sea Shells or Sensation) — tall, airy, wild look
2. **Around and between live plants:** Scatter zinnias (any mix you want — Tequila Lime, California Giant, whatever feels right)
3. **Front edge along the road:** Scatter bachelor buttons (Blue Boy) and nasturtium (Alaska Mix)
4. **Ground level gaps:** Scatter creeping thyme seed — it'll fill in as living mulch over the season
5. Press all seeds into soil surface, water gently. Don't bury deep — most are surface sowers

**Phase 5: Herbs (optional, adds to the cottage feel)**
Buy 1 rosemary plant (gallon, ~$8) and 1 lavender plant (gallon, ~$8) for instant herbal structure. Plant one on each side of the mailbox as anchors. These are evergreen/semi-evergreen and will give the bed year-round presence even in winter.

**Phase 6: Ongoing**
- Water deeply 2-3x/week for the first month while live plants establish
- After that, water only during dry spells — these are all drought-tolerant once established
- Deadhead zinnias and cosmos to keep them blooming (or don't — they'll self-seed either way)
- Let bachelor buttons and nasturtiums go to seed — they'll come back next year on their own
- By year 2: the perennials will be twice as big, the annuals will self-seed into the gaps, and the whole thing starts to look wild and intentional
- By year 3: you barely need to touch it. Just pull anything that doesn't belong and scatter fresh zinnia/cosmos seed if you want more color
