## Health Predispositions: What "Increased Risk" Actually Means

This is the section that causes the most anxiety and the most misunderstanding.

### The Key Concept: Risk ≠ Destiny

When 23andMe says "increased risk," it means your DNA has variants **associated** with higher rates of a condition in large population studies. It does NOT mean you will get it.

| Your Increased Risk | What It Actually Means | What to Do |
|-------------------|----------------------|-----------|
| **Age-Related Macular Degeneration** | You have genetic variants associated with higher risk of AMD later in life | Get regular eye exams (yearly after 40). Tell your eye doctor about this. Eat leafy greens (lutein). Don't smoke. Wear UV-protective sunglasses. |
| **Bipolar Disorder** | Genetic variants associated with increased risk. You don't have it — but the genetics are there | Be aware if mood episodes (sustained highs or lows lasting weeks, not hours) emerge. This is informational, not diagnostic. Your current GAD diagnosis accounts for your anxiety symptoms. |
| **Lupus (SLE)** | Variants associated with higher risk of autoimmune lupus | Know the signs: butterfly facial rash, joint pain, fatigue, sun sensitivity. Mention to your provider if you develop unexplained joint pain or rashes. |
| **Skin Cancer (Basal Cell)** | Higher risk, especially given your light hair/skin/freckles genetic profile | Wear sunscreen daily (even cloudy days). Annual skin checks. Watch moles for changes (ABCDE: Asymmetry, Border, Color, Diameter, Evolution). |

### "Typical Likelihood" = Not Elevated
Your risk for asthma, coronary artery disease, depression, and Type 2 diabetes is **average** — no higher than the general population. You're already managing anxiety and have good blood sugar. Keep doing what you're doing.

### "No Variants Detected" = Clear

## Pharmacogenetics: The Most Immediately Useful Section

This is the part that directly affects your medical care **right now**.

### CYP2C19: Intermediate Metabolizer

**What this gene does:** CYP2C19 is a liver enzyme that breaks down certain medications.

**What "intermediate metabolizer" means:** Your body processes these drugs more slowly than average. A standard dose stays in your system longer and may feel stronger.

**Medications affected:**
| Drug | How It Applies to You |
|------|---------------------|
| **Escitalopram (Lexapro)** | You're currently on 5mg. Because you're an intermediate metabolizer, this lower dose may work as effectively for you as 10mg works for a normal metabolizer. If your provider ever suggests increasing your dose, mention this. |
| **Clopidogrel (Plavix)** | Blood thinner — may be less effective for you. If ever prescribed, your provider should know your CYP2C19 status. |
| **Omeprazole (Prilosec)** | You're on this for acid reflux. CYP2C19 affects its metabolism too — you may get a stronger-than-average effect from standard doses. |

### SLCO1B1: Decreased Function

**What this means:** If you're ever prescribed **Simvastatin** (a cholesterol medication), you have a higher risk of muscle side effects (myopathy). Given your slightly elevated LDL, this could be relevant someday.

**What to do:** If cholesterol medication is ever discussed, tell your provider: "I have decreased SLCO1B1 function. Can we use an alternative to Simvastatin?" Rosuvastatin and Pravastatin are usually better options.

### How to Tell Your Provider

Most providers don't ask about pharmacogenetics. You'll need to bring it up:

**Script:** "I have 23andMe pharmacogenetic data. I'm a CYP2C19 intermediate metabolizer and have decreased SLCO1B1 function. Can we note this in my chart so it's considered for any future prescriptions?"

If they're dismissive, ask them to at least document it in your records.

## Carrier Status: What It Means for Your Kids

You tested negative for all 46+ carrier conditions screened by 23andMe. This means:

- You don't carry a single copy of any of these conditions
- **Your children cannot inherit these specific conditions from you** (they'd need a copy from both parents)
- This doesn't tell you about Sean's carrier status — only yours

If Sean also tested with 23andMe, his results would complete the picture. For most of these conditions, both parents need to be carriers for a child to be affected.
